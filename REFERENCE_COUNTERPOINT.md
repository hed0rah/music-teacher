# Counterpoint: Technique, Forms & Application

## Philosophy

Counterpoint is the art of combining independent melodic lines into a coherent whole. It is the oldest and most fundamental compositional discipline in Western music - predating harmony, predating tonality, predating notation itself (organum was counterpoint before anyone wrote it down).

The word itself reveals the concept: *punctus contra punctum* - point against point, note against note. Every note exists in two dimensions simultaneously: horizontally (as part of its own melodic line) and vertically (in harmonic relationship with the other voices). The tension between these two dimensions IS counterpoint.

Why rules exist: counterpoint rules are not arbitrary aesthetic preferences. They solve perceptual problems. Parallel fifths are avoided because they fuse voices into one perceived line, destroying independence. Dissonances are prepared and resolved because unprepared dissonance jolts the listener out of following individual lines. Every rule serves the goal: **audible independence of simultaneous voices**.

Constraint-driven at every level: the stricter the contrapuntal constraint (canon, fugue, invertible counterpoint), the more creative the solutions. Bach's canonic variations on "Vom Himmel Hoch" are among his greatest works precisely because the canonic constraint forces extraordinary harmonic and melodic invention.

## Species Counterpoint (Fux's Framework)

Johann Joseph Fux codified Palestrinian counterpoint in *Gradus ad Parnassum* (1725). Five species of increasing complexity, each building on the last. Still the standard pedagogical framework 300 years later. All species begin with a given melody (cantus firmus) against which the student writes a counterpoint.

### First Species: Note Against Note

One note of counterpoint for each note of cantus firmus. The simplest and most fundamental.

**Rules**:
- begin and end on perfect consonances (unison, P5, P8)
- all intervals between voices must be consonant (P1, m3, M3, P5, m6, M6, P8)
- no parallel P5 or P8 (voices fuse, independence lost)
- no direct/hidden P5 or P8 (both voices move in same direction to a perfect interval)
- contrary motion preferred; similar motion acceptable if not to perfect interval
- oblique motion (one voice stays, other moves) always safe
- no voice crossing (upper voice goes below lower voice)
- no unisons except at beginning and end
- melodic line should be mostly stepwise with occasional leaps
- leaps larger than a 4th should be followed by stepwise motion in opposite direction
- avoid tritone outlines (three or four consecutive notes outlining an augmented 4th)

**Why it matters**: first species teaches the fundamental skill of hearing two lines simultaneously. If you can't write good first species, nothing that follows will work.

### Second Species: Two Notes Against One

Two notes of counterpoint for each note of cantus firmus. Introduces the concept of strong and weak beats.

**Rules** (additions to first species):
- strong beats (downbeats): consonant with cantus firmus (same rules as first species)
- weak beats (offbeats): may be dissonant IF approached and left by step (passing tone)
- weak-beat dissonance = passing tone only (stepwise approach, stepwise departure, same direction)
- no parallel P5 or P8 on consecutive strong beats (even if intervening note breaks the parallel)
- begin with a half-rest in the counterpoint (the counterpoint enters on the weak beat)
- unisons permitted only on weak beats (not strong beats, except first and last)

**What it teaches**: rhythmic independence. The counterpoint now moves twice as fast as the cantus firmus. The listener perceives two distinct rhythmic identities.

### Third Species: Four Notes Against One

Four notes of counterpoint for each note of cantus firmus. Greater melodic fluidity.

**Rules** (additions):
- first beat: consonant with cantus firmus
- beats 2, 3, 4: may be dissonant if passing (stepwise through) or as neighbor tone (step away and step back to same note)
- cambiata (changing tone): a specific dissonant figure - step down from consonance, leap of a 3rd down, step up to consonance. A characteristic Renaissance figure.
- no consecutive strong-beat parallel P5 or P8
- melodic line should be predominantly stepwise at this speed
- avoid sequences (repeating melodic patterns) for more than 2-3 iterations

**What it teaches**: melodic fluency. Writing a singable, interesting line that moves four times faster than the anchor voice.

### Fourth Species: Syncopation (Suspensions)

The counterpoint is displaced by a half note - tied over the barline, creating dissonance on strong beats that resolves on weak beats. The birth of the suspension.

**The suspension pattern**:
1. **preparation**: consonance on weak beat (the note is established as consonant)
2. **suspension**: same note held (tied) into strong beat where it becomes dissonant against the moving cantus firmus
3. **resolution**: step down on weak beat to consonance

**Permitted suspensions** (interval on strong beat -> resolution):
- 7-6 suspension: dissonant 7th resolves down to consonant 6th
- 4-3 suspension: dissonant 4th resolves down to consonant 3rd
- 9-8 suspension: dissonant 9th resolves down to octave (use sparingly)
- 2-1 suspension: in lower voice (bass suspension), resolves down to unison

**What it teaches**: controlled dissonance. The most expressive element in Renaissance/Baroque music. Chains of 7-6 or 4-3 suspensions create the aching, yearning quality of Palestrina and Bach. This is where counterpoint becomes emotionally powerful.

### Fifth Species: Florid Counterpoint

Combines all previous species freely. The counterpoint uses a mixture of whole notes, half notes, quarter notes, and suspensions against the cantus firmus.

**Guidelines**:
- mix rhythmic values for variety and expressiveness
- use suspensions for climactic moments
- predominantly stepwise with well-placed leaps
- build toward a melodic climax (usually one highest point per phrase)
- begin simply, increase activity, return to simplicity
- all dissonance rules from previous species still apply

**What it teaches**: musical judgment. When to use a suspension vs a passing tone, when to increase rhythmic activity, when to rest. Fifth species is where counterpoint becomes composition.

## Canonic Techniques

A canon is strict imitation: one voice (the leader/dux) is followed by another (the follower/comes) reproducing the same melody at a fixed time delay and pitch interval. The strictest form of counterpoint - the composer has essentially half the freedom, since the follower's part is predetermined by the leader.

### Canon Types by Interval

**Canon at the unison**: follower enters on the same pitch as leader. The simplest canon to hear but difficult to write well - the same melody must harmonize with itself at a time delay.

**Canon at the 5th** (above or below): follower enters a 5th higher or lower. Very common - the 5th relationship mirrors the dominant-tonic relationship of tonal music. Bach uses this extensively.

**Canon at the octave**: follower enters an octave higher or lower. Similar to unison but with register separation, making voices easier to distinguish.

**Canon at the 4th, 3rd, 2nd, 6th, 7th**: all possible, each creating different harmonic relationships. Canon at the 2nd or 7th is the most challenging - the close interval creates constant potential for harsh dissonance.

### Canon Types by Transformation

**Augmentation canon**: follower plays the melody in longer note values (typically doubled). One voice moves fast, the other slow. Bach's Vom Himmel Hoch Variation IV uses this - the chorale appears in augmented values in the bass while canonic voices move above in normal rhythm.

**Diminution canon**: opposite of augmentation. Follower plays in shorter note values (typically halved). Creates acceleration effect.

**Retrograde (crab) canon / cancrizans**: follower plays the melody backwards. Notation curiosity in Renaissance; becomes structurally important in serial music.

**Inversion (mirror) canon**: follower plays the melody upside down - every ascending interval becomes a descending interval of the same size. Requires careful planning: what harmonizes going up must also harmonize going down.

**Retrograde inversion**: melody played backwards AND upside down. All four forms (prime, retrograde, inversion, retrograde-inversion) become foundational in twelve-tone technique - Schoenberg formalized what Bach did intuitively.

### Canon Types by Structure

**Perpetual canon (round)**: the canon never ends; it circles back to the beginning. "Row, Row, Row Your Boat" is a perpetual canon at the unison. Technically infinite.

**Spiral canon**: each repetition shifts up or down by a fixed interval, gradually modulating through keys. Bach's Musical Offering contains a famous spiral canon that ascends by a whole step with each repetition, cycling through 6 keys and returning to the start.

**Double canon**: two independent canons happening simultaneously (4 voices, 2 canonic pairs). Triple canon: 6 voices, 3 pairs.

**Mensuration canon**: all voices sing the same melody simultaneously but at different speeds (different time signatures). Ockeghem's Missa Prolationum: each voice reads the same notation but in a different mensuration, creating staggered versions of the same melody at 4 different speeds.

**Puzzle canon (riddle canon)**: the notation gives only the leader's part. The performer must figure out when the follower enters, at what interval, and what transformation applies. Bach and his contemporaries used these as intellectual games.

**Accompanied canon**: canonic voices over a free (non-canonic) bass or accompaniment. More practical for tonal music since the free voice can handle harmonic problems the canon creates.

### Writing Canons - Practical Approach

1. **choose interval and time delay first** - these constrain everything that follows
2. **write both voices simultaneously** - don't write the leader and hope it works; write a few notes of leader, then check what the follower produces, adjust, continue
3. **watch the vertical intervals** - every note in the leader will reappear in the follower; it must be consonant with whatever the leader is doing at that future point
4. **use the free notes wisely** - in an accompanied canon, the free bass can save you from harmonic problems
5. **begin simply** - stepwise motion in the leader creates manageable intervals when the follower enters; large leaps create unpredictable harmonic situations

## Invertible Counterpoint

Invertible counterpoint means two (or more) contrapuntal voices are designed so they can swap positions - what was on top goes to the bottom, and vice versa - and still produce correct counterpoint. This is essential for fugue writing, where the subject and countersubject must work in either position.

### Double Counterpoint at the Octave

The most common type. Two voices are written so that when one is transposed up or down by an octave, the counterpoint still works.

**Interval transformation** (when inverting at the octave):
- unison <-> octave
- 2nd <-> 7th
- 3rd <-> 6th
- 4th <-> 5th
- 5th <-> 4th
- 6th <-> 3rd

**Key implication**: the 5th (consonant) becomes a 4th (dissonant against bass in traditional counterpoint). Therefore, **avoid 5ths in double counterpoint at the octave** - they become problematic 4ths when inverted. Use 3rds, 6ths, and passing dissonances instead.

### Double Counterpoint at the 10th

Less common but creates richer sonority. One voice transposed by a 10th (octave + 3rd).

**Interval transformation**:
- 3rd <-> octave
- 5th <-> 6th
- 6th <-> 5th
- 8th <-> 3rd

**Advantage**: 5ths and 6ths exchange, both consonant. More intervals are usable than at the octave. Produces fuller harmony.

**Disadvantage**: parallel 3rds in original become parallel octaves when inverted. Must be handled carefully.

### Double Counterpoint at the 12th

One voice transposed by a 12th (octave + 5th).

**Interval transformation**:
- 3rd <-> 10th
- 5th <-> octave
- 6th <-> 7th (problematic)
- 8th <-> 5th

**Key issue**: 6ths become 7ths (dissonant) when inverted. Avoid 6ths or treat them as passing tones only.

**Advantage**: Bach uses this extensively in the Well-Tempered Clavier. Creates powerful bass transpositions of countersubjects.

### Triple and Quadruple Counterpoint

Three or four voices designed to work in any vertical arrangement. Triple counterpoint: 3 voices = 6 possible arrangements. Quadruple: 4 voices = 24 possible arrangements.

Extremely difficult to write. Bach achieves it in the Art of Fugue. The constraint is severe: every interval between every pair of voices must remain consonant in all permutations. This typically means relying heavily on 3rds, 6ths, and stepwise dissonance treatment.

## Fugue

The fugue is the most developed contrapuntal form. Not a fixed form like sonata form - more a set of procedures and conventions. No two fugues are structurally identical, but all share core elements.

### Anatomy of a Fugue

**Subject**: the main theme. Usually 1-4 bars. Establishes key, character, and melodic identity. A good subject has: clear tonal identity (establishes key quickly), distinctive rhythm, a mix of steps and leaps for character, and enough simplicity to remain recognizable through transformation.

**Answer**: the subject restated in the dominant key (a 5th higher or 4th lower).
- **real answer**: exact transposition to the dominant. Every interval preserved.
- **tonal answer**: modified transposition. The opening interval is adjusted to maintain tonal stability (typically, a subject beginning with scale degree 1-5 is answered with 5-1, not 5-2). The tonal answer prevents the music from modulating too far too fast. Required when the subject emphasizes the dominant note prominently at its opening.

**Countersubject**: a secondary melody that accompanies the answer (and subsequent entries). Written in invertible counterpoint with the subject so it can appear above or below it. A strong countersubject has contrasting rhythm to the subject (if subject uses long notes, countersubject uses shorter notes) and a distinct melodic character.

**Codetta/bridge/link**: short connective passage between entries, modulating to prepare the next voice's entry key.

### Fugal Exposition

The exposition presents the subject in each voice:

**3-voice fugue** (standard):
1. voice 1: subject in tonic
2. voice 2: answer in dominant (voice 1 continues with countersubject)
3. voice 3: subject in tonic (voices 1 & 2 continue with countersubject and free counterpoint)

**4-voice fugue**:
1. voice 1: subject (tonic)
2. voice 2: answer (dominant), voice 1: countersubject
3. voice 3: subject (tonic), voices 1-2: countersubject + free counterpoint
4. voice 4: answer (dominant), voices 1-3: countersubjects + free counterpoint

Entry order is typically from highest to lowest or lowest to highest, but not required. Bach varies entry order for dramatic effect.

### Episodes

Passages between subject entries. Episodes do NOT contain the complete subject. They:
- modulate to new keys for subsequent entries
- develop fragments (motifs) from the subject or countersubject
- provide contrast and breathing room
- often use sequences (a melodic pattern repeated at different pitch levels)
- may use imitation of subject fragments between voices

Episodes prevent monotony. A fugue that is nothing but subject entries is relentless and harmonically static.

### Middle Entries

Subject entries after the exposition, typically in related keys:
- relative major/minor
- subdominant
- supertonic
- other closely related keys
- remote keys for dramatic effect (later in the fugue)

Each middle entry is separated by episodes. The tonal journey of a fugue: tonic -> dominant -> exploration of related keys -> return to tonic.

### Fugal Devices

**Stretto**: subject entries that overlap - the next voice begins the subject before the previous voice has finished it. Creates compression, urgency, climactic intensity. Stretto typically appears later in the fugue, building toward climax. Multiple stretto entries simultaneously = maximum contrapuntal density.

**Augmentation**: subject played in longer note values (usually doubled). Creates gravitas, weight. Often in the bass near the end of a fugue.

**Diminution**: subject in shorter note values (usually halved). Creates energy, acceleration.

**Inversion**: subject played upside down (ascending intervals become descending, vice versa). Creates mirror-image relationship.

**Retrograde**: subject played backwards. Rare in tonal fugues (more common in serial music). When combined with inversion: retrograde-inversion.

**Pedal point**: sustained note (usually tonic or dominant) held in one voice while other voices continue the counterpoint above. Typically appears near the end. Dominant pedal creates tension before final tonic resolution. Tonic pedal establishes finality.

**False entry**: beginning of the subject that breaks off after a few notes. Creates expectation without fulfillment. Useful in episodes.

### Types of Fugue

**Double fugue**: two subjects, introduced either simultaneously from the start or in separate expositions that eventually combine. The Art of Fugue Contrapunctus IX: double fugue where both subjects are invertible.

**Triple fugue**: three subjects. Bach's Fugue in C# minor (WTC I): three subjects gradually introduced, ultimately combined in triple counterpoint.

**Permutation fugue**: all voices always state the subject or a derivative. No free counterpoint. Extremely strict.

**Fughetta**: short fugue, often just exposition + brief development.

**Fugato**: fugal passage within a non-fugal work (e.g., development section of a symphony begins with a fugato on the main theme).

**Ricercar**: older, more academic fugue. Longer subjects, slower-moving, more learned in character. Bach's Musical Offering ricercar: the most complex fugal writing in existence.

### Writing Fugues - Practical Approach

1. **write the subject first** - it determines everything. Test it: can you sing it? Does it have rhythmic character? Does it establish the key clearly?
2. **write the countersubject simultaneously with the answer** - these must work in invertible counterpoint. Test inversion immediately.
3. **plan the tonal journey** - map out which keys you'll visit and in what order before writing episodes
4. **use stretto as climactic tool** - check which stretto intervals are possible (can the subject overlap with itself at a 1-beat delay? 2-beat? At the 5th? Octave?). Not all subjects allow stretto - design the subject with stretto potential if you want it
5. **end with weight** - pedal point, augmentation in bass, stretto pile-up, or combination. The final tonic entry should feel inevitable

## Other Contrapuntal Forms

### Invention (2-voice)

Bach's 15 Two-Part Inventions: models of 2-voice counterpoint. Short (typically 20-30 bars), based on a single motif, exploring imitation, sequence, and modulation. The pedagogical bridge between species counterpoint and fugue.

**Structure**: announcement of motif in one voice, imitation in the other, modulation through related keys, return to tonic. Simpler than fugue: no formal exposition/episode structure, more free-flowing.

### Sinfonia (3-voice invention)

Bach's 15 Three-Part Sinfonias (sometimes called Three-Part Inventions). Same principle as inventions but with three independent voices. The step between 2-voice counterpoint and full fugue writing.

### Passacaglia

Repeated bass line (ground bass / basso ostinato), typically 4-8 bars, over which continuous variations unfold. Contrapuntal because the upper voices must create independent lines against the fixed bass.

Bach's Passacaglia in C minor (BWV 582): 20 variations + fugue on the same 8-bar bass theme. The bass eventually migrates to upper voices and becomes the fugue subject. Webern orchestrated the ricercar from Musical Offering; Stravinsky arranged these Vom Himmel Hoch variations - both attracted to Bach's contrapuntal architecture.

### Chaconne

Similar to passacaglia but the repeating element is a chord progression rather than a specific bass line. The bass may vary while the harmonic pattern remains fixed.

Bach's Chaconne from Partita No. 2 in D minor (BWV 1004): 256 bars for solo violin, one of the greatest works in Western music. Single instrument sustains counterpoint, harmony, and virtuosity simultaneously.

### Quodlibet

Combination of two or more pre-existing melodies simultaneously. The Goldberg Variations finale: Bach combines two folk songs in counterpoint over the aria's bass. The ultimate demonstration that different melodies can coexist in a single contrapuntal texture.

## Counterpoint in Different Historical Contexts

### Renaissance Counterpoint (Palestrina Style)

- predominantly stepwise motion
- dissonance only as passing tone or suspension (species 2 and 4 rules)
- voices equally important (no hierarchy)
- smooth, balanced, vocal writing
- text clarity paramount
- imitative entries standard
- modal (not yet tonal)

### Baroque Counterpoint (Bach Style)

- tonal framework: counterpoint within functional harmony
- more rhythmic independence between voices
- harmonic rhythm drives the counterpoint (chord changes create structural moments)
- sequences used extensively (motif repeated at different pitch levels)
- chromaticism more common (secondary dominants, modulation)
- instrumental counterpoint: wider leaps, faster passagework than Renaissance vocal style
- fugue as supreme form

### Classical/Romantic Counterpoint

- counterpoint serves harmonic structure (reversed relationship from Baroque)
- fugato passages within sonata-form movements (Beethoven development sections)
- contrapuntal texture used for climactic intensity or "learned" character
- Brahms: late Romantic mastery of counterpoint within Romantic harmonic language
- not the primary compositional method but a tool within a larger toolkit

### 20th Century Counterpoint

**Hindemith**: systematic approach to modern counterpoint. *Ludus Tonalis* (1942): preludes, interludes, fugues in his own modal/chromatic tonal system. Counterpoint textbook (*The Craft of Musical Composition*) updates Fux for modern harmony.

**Bartok**: folk-derived counterpoint. Modal lines interact creating harmonic clashes that are both folk-authentic and modernist. Night music textures: multiple independent lines creating atmospheric counterpoint (insect-like, nocturnal).

**Shostakovich**: 24 Preludes and Fugues Op. 87 (after hearing Richter play WTC). Tonal fugues with 20th-century harmonic language: chromatic subjects, dissonant countersubjects, distorted tonality. Some fugues are devastatingly simple; others are brutally complex. The DSCH motif appears as fugue subject.

**Serial counterpoint**: the twelve-tone row and its transformations (P, I, R, RI) function as contrapuntal voices. Webern: extreme economy, each voice a row form, crystalline clarity through serialized counterpoint. The row IS the contrapuntal subject.

**Ligeti's micropolyphony**: many voices (sometimes 20-30+) each with independent lines, moving so densely that individual voices dissolve into texture. Counterpoint where the result is not audible polyphony but slowly shifting harmonic mass. *Atmospheres*: 56 string parts, each independent, creating collective texture. The opposite of audible counterpoint - counterpoint used to CREATE texture.

### Stravinsky and Bach

Stravinsky's arrangements of Bach (Vom Himmel Hoch variations, 1956; orchestrations of canonic works) reveal his deep engagement with counterpoint. During his transition into serialism, Stravinsky studied Bach's contrapuntal techniques as a bridge to twelve-tone thinking. His orchestrations translate organ registration into instrumental color: each contrapuntal voice gets a distinct timbral identity, making the counterpoint more audible than on the organ itself. Orchestration as contrapuntal revelation.

## Rules Summary: Quick Reference

### Melodic Rules (per voice)
- predominantly stepwise (conjunct) motion
- leaps followed by stepwise motion in opposite direction
- avoid augmented intervals melodically
- avoid outlining a tritone across consecutive notes
- one climax point per phrase
- leading tone resolves up to tonic
- avoid more than 3 consecutive leaps

### Harmonic Rules (between voices)
- begin on perfect consonance (P1, P5, P8)
- end on perfect consonance
- no parallel P5 or P8 (destroys independence)
- no direct/hidden P5 or P8 in outer voices (both voices leap in same direction to perfect interval)
- prefer contrary motion
- 3rds and 6ths are the safest intervals (always consonant, don't fuse voices)
- P4 treated as dissonance against bass; consonant between upper voices
- dissonance must be prepared (approached by step or tied over) and resolved (step down, typically)

### Independence Rules
- rhythmic contrast between voices (when one moves fast, the other moves slow)
- melodic contrast (different contour shapes)
- stagger climax points (voices shouldn't peak simultaneously)
- avoid extended parallel motion (more than 3 beats of parallel 3rds or 6ths sounds like one thickened voice, not two independent lines)
- contrary motion at cadences (creates strongest sense of two independent voices arriving at the same point)
