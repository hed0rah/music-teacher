# Music Theory Reference: Harmony, Voice-Leading, and Form

## Harmonic Systems

### Functional Tonality (Tonal Harmony)

**Foundation**: All harmony relates to a tonal center (tonic = home). Harmonic function creates expectation and resolution.

**Three Main Functions**:

1. **Tonic (I)**: Home, rest, resolution. Stable, establishes key.
2. **Subdominant (IV, ii)**: "Preparation," movement away from tonic. Sets up expectation.
3. **Dominant (V, VII)**: Tension, "requiring resolution." Pulls back to tonic.

**Basic Harmonic Progression**:
- I → IV → V → I (Tonic → Subdominant → Dominant → Tonic)
- This 4-bar progression = fundamental progression underlying much tonal music
- Variations possible but relationships remain

**Voice-Leading Rules in Functional Tonality**:
1. Common tones: Share notes between consecutive chords when possible (reduces awkward jumps)
2. Stepwise motion: Voices move by second when possible (creates smooth, vocal-like quality)
3. Contrary motion: Outer voices (soprano/bass) move in opposite directions (creates balance)
4. Avoid parallel fifths/octaves between outer voices (creates hollow sound, traditionally avoided)
5. Leading tone resolution: 7th scale degree pulls to tonic (8th scale degree)
6. Resolution of tendency tones: 4th scale degree pulls down to 3rd, 7th pulls to 8th

**Harmonic Rhythm**: Speed at which chords change
- Slow harmonic rhythm (chords change infrequently) = stability, broadness
- Fast harmonic rhythm (chords change frequently) = activity, instability

**Cadences** (harmonic phrases):

1. **Authentic Cadence** (V → I): 
   - "True" cadence, creates sense of arrival
   - Strongest harmonic conclusion
   - Half-cadence variant (ending on V) creates suspension

2. **Plagal Cadence** (IV → I):
   - "Amen cadence" (from hymn tradition)
   - Weaker than authentic, creates finality without drama
   - Often used for final bars

3. **Deceptive Cadence** (V → vi):
   - Subverts expectation (V usually resolves to I, not vi)
   - Creates surprise, temporary evasion of resolution
   - Used for dramatic effect

4. **Phrygian Cadence** (iv → I):
   - Minor version of plagal
   - Creates darker, religious quality

### Extended Functional Harmony

**Secondary Dominants**: Dominant function applied to non-tonic chords
- V/ii creates temporary pull to ii chord
- V/IV creates temporary pull to IV chord
- Creates harmonic interest, temporary tonal shifts without modulation
- Very common in late Romantic music

**Modulation**: Change of tonal center
- Through closely-related keys (keys sharing most notes)
- Via pivot chord (single chord that functions in both keys)
- Via enharmonic respelling (same note spelled differently)
- Creates large-scale form and variety

### Chromatic Harmony

**Chromatic Alteration**: Adding notes outside the diatonic scale
- Raised 4th scale degree (creating tritone with root)
- Lowered 7th scale degree
- Augmented 6th chords (German, Italian, French variants)
- Creates tension, instability, richness

**Augmented 6th Chords**:
- German Augmented Sixth: iv6/5 with raised 4th (most common)
- Italian Augmented Sixth: iv6 with raised 4th (simplest)
- French Augmented Sixth: iv with both raised 4th and 6th scale degrees
- All resolve to V (dominant preparation)
- Creates harmonic urgency and excitement

### Atonal/Chromatic Harmony

**Chromatic Saturation**: All 12 pitches continuously present
- No tonal center (no "home" feeling)
- All pitches equally important
- Creates constant mild dissonance
- No functional progression (movement toward specific goal)
- Ligeti's signature approach
- Creates unease, timelessness, rejection of tonal pull

**Serial/Twelve-Tone Organization**:
- Predetermined arrangement of all 12 chromatic pitches (the "row")
- All harmony derived from row permutations
- Ensures all pitches used equally
- Creates systematic order without tonal hierarchy
- Four forms available: Prime, Inversion, Retrograde, Retrograde-Inversion
- Webern's mastery: symmetrical rows create perceptual equivalences

**Pitch Class Set Theory**: Abstract approach treating pitch relationships mathematically
- Labels pitch classes (C, C♯, D, etc.) without octave designation
- Analyzes intervallic content and transformations
- Used in analysis of atonal, serial, and contemporary music
- Describes pitch relationships independent of registration

### Spectral Harmony

**Foundation**: Analyze harmonic spectrum of complex sounds (bells, gongs, voices) and use harmonic series relationships as compositional basis.

**Harmonic Series**: Natural overtone series above fundamental
- 1st partial = fundamental (F)
- 2nd partial = octave above fundamental (F)
- 3rd partial = perfect fifth above 2nd partial (C, a fifth above the octave)
- 4th partial = 2nd octave (F)
- 5th partial = major third above 4th partial (A)
- 6th partial = perfect fifth above 4th partial (C)
- And so on...

**Spectral Harmony Application**:
1. Record complex sound (bell, tam-tam, voice)
2. Analyze using FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) to determine frequency content
3. Identify all frequency components and their relative amplitudes
4. Compose by assigning those frequencies to orchestral instruments
5. Result: orchestration that echoes timbre of original sound

**Why It Works**: 
- Orchestration mimics real acoustic resonance
- Creates harmonic justification for otherwise unusual chords
- Opens composition to acoustic physics as primary material
- Grisey, Murail, Scelsi (unknowingly) developed this approach

**Spectral Chord Examples**:
- C bell spectrum might contain: C, C, G, C, E, G, B♭, C, D, E, F♯, G...
- Composing that spectrum creates "bell-chord" reflecting bell's actual harmonic content
- Different from traditional functional chords, justified by acoustic reality

### Cluster Harmony

**Concept**: All notes within defined pitch range = single harmonic unit (a "cluster")

**Definition**: C to E♭ cluster = all pitches C, C♯, D, D♯, E♭ played simultaneously as single sonority
- Not individual notes in harmony
- Cluster = timbre, density, not melody/harmony in traditional sense

**Transposition**: Clusters move through pitch space
- C cluster moves to D♭ cluster (all pitches shift up semitone)
- Creates motion without traditional voice-leading

**Density Changes**: Widen or narrow cluster
- Wider cluster (larger pitch range) = more massive sound
- Narrower cluster = more intense, concentrated sound
- Can grow or shrink over time for dramatic effect

**Orchestration of Clusters**: All instruments in range play simultaneously
- No individual voice independence
- Timbre = combination of all instruments' timbres at that register
- Used in Penderecki, Xenakis

**Harmonic Function**: Unlike traditional chords, clusters don't have functional relationships
- Not consonance/dissonance in traditional sense
- Judged by acoustic effect, density, register, not harmonic function
- Creates texture rather than harmony in traditional sense

### Microtonal Harmony

**Pitch Divisions**: Beyond equal temperament's 12 equal divisions per octave

**Quarter-Tones**: 24 equal divisions per octave (24 pitches per octave)
- Double the chromatic resolution
- Notation: sharps, flats, and additional symbols
- Creates subtle pitch variations
- Heard as "almost in tune but not quite" when near equal temperament pitches
- Beating effects when combined with equal temperament pitches

**Sixth-Tones**: 36 divisions per octave
- Even finer pitch control
- Used in some contemporary music and non-Western traditions
- Requires specialized notation

**Just Intonation**: Pitch ratios based on harmonic series (rather than equal temperament)
- 5/4 interval = major third (purer than equal temperament)
- 7/4 interval = septimal minor seventh (darker than equal temperament)
- Creates acoustic consonance through pure frequency ratios
- Requires retuning between pieces/sections
- Scelsi explored this; some spectral composers use it

**Microtonal Harmony Effects**:
- Beating: Two pitches slightly detuned create periodic volume variation (interference pattern)
- Creates uncanny quality: sounds almost in tune but unsettling
- Harmonic richness from non-12-ET pitch relationships
- Used extensively in Ligeti (subtle microtonality), Xenakis, spectral music

## Voice-Leading Principles

### Four-Part Vocal Writing (SATB: Soprano, Alto, Tenor, Bass)

**Traditional Principles**:

1. **Range Limitations**:
   - Soprano: C4 to G5 (comfortable); C4 to A5 (extended)
   - Alto: G3 to D5 (comfortable); G3 to F5 (extended)
   - Tenor: C3 to A4 (comfortable); C3 to C5 (extended)
   - Bass: F2 to D4 (comfortable); A1 to F4 (extended)

2. **Common Tones**: Keep shared pitches between chords in same voice (smoothness)

3. **Stepwise Motion**: Move voices by second when possible (creates singability)

4. **Contrary Motion**: Outer voices (soprano/bass) move opposite directions (balance, avoids parallel perfects)

5. **Voice Crossing**: Avoid when possible (creates confusing voice independence); acceptable in inner voices (alto/tenor)

6. **Parallel Fifths/Octaves**: Avoid between outer voices (sounds hollow); acceptable between inner voices in modern style

7. **Hidden Fifths/Octaves**: Soprano and bass leap in similar motion to perfect fifth/octave (less problematic than parallel if soprano moves by leap)

### Orchestral Voice-Leading (Modified Traditional Rules)

**Key Difference**: Orchestral writing has more freedom than vocal writing
- Voices need not maintain individual independence
- Blend and balance more important than voice continuity
- Can use wider ranges, more dramatic leaps
- Timbre substitution (transferring melodic line between instruments) often preferred

**Orchestral Principles**:

1. **Timbre Continuity**: Transfer melodic line between instruments that blend well (creates seamless transition)

2. **Register Balance**: Maintain relatively even distribution across registers (avoids clustering all notes high or low)

3. **Doubling**: Double important voices (tonic, melody) for strength; avoid doubling leading tone or tritone (creates confusion)

4. **Avoid Octave Gaps**: In sustained passages, don't leave awkward octave gaps between active voices (creates thinness)

5. **Inner Voices**: Least exposed, can be less carefully managed; fill harmonic space rather than carry melodic content

6. **Outer Voices**: Most exposed; soprano (highest) and bass (lowest) carry most of structural weight

### Voice-Leading in Non-Functional Harmony

**Serial Music Voice-Leading**:
- Traditional voice-leading rules difficult to apply (no functional relationships)
- Webern's approach: use the row's internal symmetries to create voice independence
- Symmetrical rows facilitate counterpoint (mirror relationships create natural voice separation)
- Focus on pitch class (not specific octave), use registral variety for color

**Spectral Music Voice-Leading**:
- Voice-leading emerges from acoustic relationships
- Voices follow partials' amplitude and frequency relationships
- Less about independent voices, more about ensemble resonance
- Cluster harmony (voices in similar ranges) intentional, not avoidance

**Graphic Score Voice-Leading**:
- Visual organization replaces traditional notation
- Spatial relationships indicate timing and duration
- Voice independence less relevant; texture/density more important
- Performer interpretation replaces strict voice-leading rules

## Form & Structure

### Sectional Forms

**Phrase**: Smallest structural unit (4-8 bars typically)
- Opens with question (antecedent phrase)
- Closes with answer (consequent phrase)
- Creates musical sentence

**Period**: Two phrases creating complete thought
- Antecedent phrase (ends on V or HC half-cadence)
- Consequent phrase (ends on I or authentic cadence)
- Creates closure and completion

**Binary Form (AB)**:
- Two distinct sections
- First section: moves to related key or dominant
- Second section: returns to tonic
- A section: exposition
- B section: development + recapitulation
- Simple, clear structure (minuets, baroque dance forms)

**Ternary Form (ABA)**:
- Three sections
- A: exposition
- B: contrasting section (different key, theme, character)
- A: return to opening (usually modified or shortened)
- Creates broader arc than binary
- Used in minuets, songs, slower movements

**Rondo Form (ABACA or ABACABA)**:
- Recurring principal section (A) alternates with contrasting episodes
- A always returns in tonic
- B and C episodes modulate or explore related keys
- Creates sense of return and cyclical structure
- Common in final movements

### Sonata Form

**Structure**:
1. **Exposition**: Introduces primary (tonic) and secondary (dominant/related key) themes
   - Primary theme in tonic key
   - Transition modulates to related key
   - Secondary theme in new key
   - Closing theme

2. **Development**: Explores and fragments themes from exposition
   - Modulates through various keys
   - Combines, inverts, develops themes
   - Creates instability and expectation of return
   - Usually longest, most complex section

3. **Recapitulation**: Returns to exposition material but in tonic key
   - Primary theme in tonic
   - Transition (modified, stays in tonic)
   - Secondary theme in tonic (key change)
   - Closing theme in tonic
   - Restored stability

**Why It Matters**:
- Fundamental form for symphonies, concertos, chamber music
- Creates dramatic arc: establishment → exploration → return
- Psychological: expectation (exposition), disruption (development), resolution (recapitulation)
- Allows thematic development over large spans

**Modifications in Practice**:
- Slow introductions precede exposition
- Codas (concluding passages) extend ending
- Exposition often repeated (creates familiarity)
- Development can extend far beyond exposition length
- Thematic material can be fragmented beyond recognition

### Theme & Variations

**Structure**:
- Statement of theme (clear, memorable)
- Variation 1: melody ornamented
- Variation 2: melody in different register
- Variation 3: rhythm changed
- And so on...
- Continuous development of single theme

**Variation Types**:
1. **Melodic variation**: Theme recognizable but ornamented (added notes, embellishment)
2. **Harmonic variation**: Melody stays but underlying chords change
3. **Rhythmic variation**: Melody rhythm altered (slower, faster, syncopated)
4. **Registral variation**: Same melody in different octave/instrument
5. **Textural variation**: Theme accompaniment changes (sparse to dense)
6. **Orchestral variation**: Theme given to different instruments

**Passacaglia/Chaconne**: Special case of variations
- **Passacaglia**: Repeated bass line (ground bass), harmony changes above it
- **Chaconne**: Repeated chord progression (not just bass)
- Creates unity through harmonic ostinato while melody/texture changes

### No Form / Free Form

**Characteristics**:
- Narrative arc rather than traditional sectional structure
- No return of material (unlike ABA, rondo, sonata)
- No recognizable formal divisions
- Form emerges from content (emotional/expressive arc) rather than structural template

**Examples**:
- Romantic-era symphonic poems (Liszt)
- Some Ligeti pieces (Atmosphères)
- Graphic score improvisations
- Aleatoric compositions without clear sections

**Compositional Strategy**:
- Establish opening gesture
- Develop and transform it
- Create arc toward climax
- Resolve or drift to ending
- Form emerges from organic growth, not predetermined template

## Formal Distortion as Expression

### Shostakovich: Distorted Sonata Form

**Technique**: Use sonata form but distort traditional relationships
- Return to primary theme in wrong key (not tonic)
- Extend development beyond expected length
- Eliminate expected sections
- Recapitulation incomplete or fragmented
- Coda extended or unexpected

**Expressive Effect**:
- Disrupted expectation = emotional disruption
- Key relationships mimic psychological unease
- Form's distortion = content's distortion
- Listener knows "something is wrong" even without analytical knowledge

**Example**: Shostakovich Symphony 4, first movement
- Expected recapitulation arrives in wrong key
- Listener sense of wrongness = compositional intent
- Form itself becomes expressive medium

### Non-Teleological Form

**Concept**: Music without goal-directed progression
- Each moment stands equally (no hierarchy)
- No climax, no direction toward resolution
- Contrast through juxtaposition rather than development
- Aleatoric, chance procedures possible

**Composers Using This**:
- Cage (pieces designed not to go anywhere)
- Stockhausen (moment form: discrete moments reorderable)
- Some minimalism (Reich: phasing pieces have no climax, just evolution)

**Expressive Potential**:
- Timelessness (no progression toward ending)
- Simultaneity (all moments equally present)
- Liberation from Western narrative arc
- Zen Buddhist aesthetic (no teleology, just "what is")

### Moment Form (Stockhausen)

**Definition**: Composition as series of discrete moments standing on equal footing

**Characteristics**:
- Moments don't necessarily connect logically
- No hierarchy (no main moment, no subordinate moment)
- Potentially reorderable (different performance orders possible)
- Each moment complete in itself
- Transitions might be abrupt or non-existent

**Compositional Advantage**:
- Abandons teleological expectation
- Creates freedom from goal-directedness
- Allows maximum contrast and surprise
- Listener present in each moment rather than anticipating climax

**Example**: Stockhausen's Momente
- Composition of discrete temporal moments
- Some moments can be interchanged
- No single "correct" path through material

## Minimal/Maximal Approaches

### Minimalism

**Characteristics**:
- Repetitive material (melodic cells, rhythmic patterns)
- Slow transformation (incremental change)
- Static harmony (often single chord)
- Hypnotic, meditative quality
- Trance-like from repetition

**Types**:

1. **Process Music** (Steve Reich):
   - Identical patterns in different instruments
   - Slight tempo differences create gradual phase shifting
   - Mechanical, inevitable process
   - Example: "Piano Phase," "Come Out"

2. **Additive Minimalism** (Philip Glass):
   - Begin with small cell
   - Add notes incrementally
   - Build phrase through addition
   - Creates growth from simplicity

3. **Repetitive Simplicity** (Erik Satie, La Monte Young):
   - Extreme repetition
   - Very simple material
   - Meditative effect
   - Rejection of complexity as depth

**Expressive Potential**:
- Meditative, hypnotic quality
- Sameness as profound (not shallow)
- Process becomes content
- Western obsession with development questioned

### Maximalism

**Characteristics**:
- Complex, dense material
- Multiple simultaneous processes
- Textural/parametral richness
- Cluster harmony, extended techniques
- Overwhelming sensory experience

**Approaches**:
- Spectral complexity (Grisey, Murail)
- Cluster density (Penderecki)
- Parametral saturation (Xenakis)
- Graphic chaos (Wolff, Cage)

**Expressive Potential**:
- Overwhelming, immersive experience
- Rejection of clarity for textural richness
- Stimulation of perception at multiple levels
- Complexity as beauty (not simplicity)

## Harmonic Rhythm & Pacing

### Fast Harmonic Rhythm
- Chords change frequently (every beat, every half-beat)
- Creates activity, instability
- Drives listener forward
- Associated with tension, excitement

### Slow Harmonic Rhythm
- Chords change infrequently (every 2-4 bars)
- Creates stability, broadness
- Allows time for individual chord color to register
- Associated with calm, contemplation

### Harmonic Rhythm Changes
- Accelerating harmonic rhythm = building intensity
- Decelerating harmonic rhythm = resolution, relaxation
- Strategic change = dramatic effect
- Used extensively in Baroque (Bach) for climactic passages

## Tonality & Atonality Spectrum

**Tonal Music**: Clear tonal center, harmonic function, expectation/resolution
**Extended Tonality**: Tonal center present but weakened, some functional harmony remains
**Atonal Music**: No tonal center, no functional harmony, all pitches equal importance
**Post-Atonal Music**: Atonal but organized (serial, spectral, set-theory based)

Music rarely exists at extremes; most falls somewhere on spectrum.
- Late Romantic = extended tonality (Wagner, Mahler)
- Debussy = ambiguous tonality (floating, no clear center)
- Schoenberg early = atonal (Second Viennese School period)
- Webern = serial atonal (highly organized)
- Ligeti = chromatic saturation (extreme atonality)
- Spectral = tonal derivation (harmony justified by acoustic spectrum)

Understanding position on spectrum helps analyze and compose effectively.
