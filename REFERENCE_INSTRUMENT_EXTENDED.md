# Extended Instrument Reference: Non-Orchestral & Contemporary

## Philosophy

These instruments are as compositionally significant as orchestral forces. The drum kit is the rhythmic engine of contemporary music. The electric guitar reshaped harmony and timbre. The synthesizer expanded the timbral palette infinitely. Vocals remain the most emotionally direct instrument. Each has extended techniques, timbral characteristics, and compositional possibilities that deserve the same depth of understanding as orchestral instruments.

---

## Drum Kit

### Components & Tuning

**Kick Drum (Bass Drum)**:
- Sizes: 18", 20", 22", 24" diameter. Larger = lower fundamental, more sustain.
- Tuning: batter head (struck) tuned for attack character. Resonant head (front) for tone/sustain. Tighter = higher pitch, more attack. Looser = lower, boomier.
- Damping: pillow/blanket inside controls sustain. More damping = punchier, tighter. Less = resonant, boomy.
- Beater type: felt (warm, round), plastic/wood (bright, clicky attack).
- Port hole in resonant head: tightens sound, allows internal mic placement.
- Frequency: fundamental 40-80Hz, beater attack 2-5kHz, sub-energy below 60Hz.

**Snare Drum**:
- Sizes: typically 14" diameter, 5-6.5" depth. Deeper = more body. Shallower = crackier.
- Shell material: maple (warm), birch (bright, focused), steel (cutting, loud), brass (warm-bright), aluminum (dry, controlled), bronze (dark, complex).
- Snare wires: metal coils on bottom head create characteristic buzz. Adjustable tension. Snares disengaged = tom-like.
- Tuning: higher = crack/pop. Lower = fat, thuddy. Top head slightly tighter than bottom = standard.
- Frequency: fundamental 150-300Hz, body 300-800Hz, crack/snap 1-5kHz, wires 5-10kHz.

**Toms**:
- Rack toms: 10", 12", 13" (mounted). Floor toms: 14", 16", 18" (on legs/stand).
- Melodic tuning: toms tuned to intervals (minor thirds, fourths, fifths) create melodic fills. Single-headed (concert toms): less sustain, more attack.
- Roto-toms: continuously tunable, used for melodic percussion.

**Hi-Hat**:
- Two cymbals on foot-pedal stand. Closed (tight tick), open (washy, sustaining), half-open (sizzly).
- Foot chick: closing hi-hat with pedal only = tight percussive click.
- Sizes: 13-15". Heavier = louder, darker. Lighter = brighter, trashier.

**Cymbals**:
- Crash: explosive, bright. 14-20". Thin = fast, washy. Thick = louder, more sustain.
- Ride: sustaining, defined. 20-24". Bell (dome) = clear ping. Body = wash. Edge = crash-like.
- China: trashy, aggressive, inverted edge. Metal/punk staple.
- Splash: small (6-12"), fast accent, quick decay.
- Effects: stacked, drilled, riveted for unusual timbres.

### Playing Techniques

**Grip**: matched (both hands identical, palms down) or traditional (left hand underhand, marching heritage, jazz standard).

**Stick types**: 5A (general purpose), 5B (heavier, rock), 7A (lighter, jazz), brushes (wire or nylon, jazz/gentle), mallets (soft attack), rods/bundles (medium volume).

**Core techniques**:
- Single stroke roll: alternating R-L-R-L. Foundation of all drumming.
- Double stroke roll: R-R-L-L. Bounced strokes. Essential for speed.
- Paradiddle: R-L-R-R L-R-L-L. Creates accent patterns from sticking.
- Flam: grace note before main stroke. Thickens attack.
- Drag: two grace notes before main stroke.
- Ghost notes: very quiet snare hits between main strokes. Defines groove complexity. The difference between beginner and professional drumming is often ghost note control.
- Rim shot: stick hits rim and head simultaneously = loud crack.
- Cross-stick (rim click): stick laid across head, butt strikes rim = woodblock-like.
- Brushwork: circular sweep on snare (jazz), taps, swishes. Intimate, textural.

**Advanced techniques**:
- Double bass: two kick pedals (or double pedal). Metal, progressive rock, jazz fusion. Independence between feet required.
- Blast beats: simultaneous alternating kick-snare at extreme speed. Grindcore, death metal, black metal.
- Linear drumming: no two limbs hit simultaneously. Creates interlocking patterns where rhythm emerges from the sequence of individual hits.
- Moeller technique: whip-like arm motion for efficient speed/power. Relaxed wrist, momentum-driven.
- Polyrhythmic independence: each limb maintaining a different rhythmic pattern. 3 against 4, 5 against 3, etc. West African polyrhythm influence.

### Electronic Drums & Drum Machines

**Drum synthesis**:
- 808 kick: sine wave with pitch envelope (pitch drops from ~200Hz to ~50Hz in ~50ms). THE most influential electronic kick sound. Sub-bass dominant.
- 808 snare: filtered noise burst + two sine wave tones. Snappy, pitched.
- 808 clap: multiple short noise bursts in rapid succession simulating multiple hands.
- 909 sounds: analog synthesis closer to acoustic emulation. Kick: bridged-T oscillator. Snare: noise + oscillator. Hi-hat: six square-wave oscillators at non-harmonic frequencies.
- FM hi-hats/cymbals: metallic spectra from inharmonic FM ratios. Operator-based synthesis for metallic percussion.

**Drum programming**:
- Step sequencing: TR-808/909 style. 16-step grid, each step = 16th note. Pattern-based.
- MIDI sequencing: piano-roll programming. More flexible than step sequencing.
- Quantization vs humanization: straight quantize = mechanical, rigid. Swing (delay certain beats) = groove. Velocity variation + micro-timing shifts = humanized feel. Push/pull: notes slightly before beat = driving, urgent. Slightly after = laid back, relaxed.
- Velocity layers: multiple samples per instrument at different hit strengths. Round-robin: cycling through slightly different samples to avoid "machine gun" effect.

## Electric Guitar

### Construction & Tone Factors

**Pickups** (electromagnetic transducers converting string vibration to electrical signal):
- Single-coil: bright, clear, articulate. Characteristic "quack" in positions 2/4 (two pickups combined out of phase). Fender Stratocaster, Telecaster. Susceptible to 60Hz electromagnetic interference (hum).
- Humbucker: two coils wired to cancel hum (hence "hum-bucking"). Warmer, thicker, higher output. Gibson Les Paul, SG. Coil-splitting: switches to single-coil mode for versatility.
- P-90: large single-coil. Gritty, raw, punchy. Between single-coil and humbucker character. Gibson original design.
- Active pickups: battery-powered preamp built into pickup. Higher output, consistent tone, lower noise. EMG (metal standard).
- Pickup position: bridge = bright, thin, cutting. Neck = warm, thick, mellow. Middle = scooped, "quacky."

**Body/construction**: solid body (sustain, no feedback issues), semi-hollow (warmer, some feedback potential), hollow body (jazz archtop, very warm, very feedback-prone).

**Scale length**: 25.5" Fender (brighter, more tension), 24.75" Gibson (warmer, easier bends, slightly less tension), 27"+ baritone (lower tunings with adequate tension).

### Techniques

**Standard**: alternate picking, economy picking, hammer-on/pull-off (legato), bending (pitch manipulation by pushing string laterally), vibrato (oscillating bend), palm muting (chunky, muted tone), power chords (root + 5th, ambiguous major/minor), barre chords.

**Extended/advanced**:
- Tapping: right-hand finger taps fretboard notes. Two-hand tapping = melodic range across entire fretboard. Eddie Van Halen popularized.
- Sweep picking: single pick stroke across multiple strings. For arpeggiated patterns. Neoclassical metal technique.
- Natural harmonics: touch string at nodal points (12th, 7th, 5th fret etc.) without pressing to fretboard. Bell-like, ethereal.
- Pinch/artificial harmonics: thumb touches string immediately after pick attack. Squealing, screaming overtone. Zakk Wylde, Billy Gibbons signature.
- Volume swelling: roll volume knob or use volume pedal to remove pick attack. Creates violin-like, ambient sustain.
- Slide guitar: glass or metal tube on finger slides along strings. Continuous pitch (no frets). Delta blues, country, ambient.
- Feedback: controlled amplifier feedback as sustained tone. Guitar position relative to speaker determines pitch/behavior. Hendrix, Sonic Youth, shoegaze.
- Prepared guitar: objects on strings, e-bows (electromagnetic sustainer creates infinite sustain without picking), altered tuning.
- Tremolo arm (whammy bar): pitch bending via bridge manipulation. Dive bombs, flutter, vibrato. Floyd Rose locking tremolo = extreme range with tuning stability.
- Alternate tunings: Drop D (DADGBE), DADGAD (Celtic/ambient), Open G (DGDGBD, Keith Richards), Open D (DADF#AD), All Fourths (EADGCF). Each enables different voicings, resonances, open-string drones.

### Effects Chain

**Standard signal chain order**: guitar -> tuner -> filter/wah -> compressor -> overdrive/distortion/fuzz -> modulation (chorus/flanger/phaser) -> delay -> reverb -> amplifier.

**Core effects**:
- Overdrive: soft clipping, amp-like breakup. Tube Screamer: mid-push, smooth saturation.
- Distortion: harder clipping, more gain/sustain. RAT, Boss DS-1. Heavier than overdrive.
- Fuzz: extreme clipping, buzzy, woolly, splattery. Big Muff (sustaining, violin-like), Fuzz Face (dynamic, responsive). Octave fuzz: adds sub-octave.
- Wah: swept bandpass filter (foot-controlled). Cry Baby. Vocal-like sweep. Envelope filter (auto-wah): filter follows picking dynamics.
- Chorus: short modulated delay creates doubling/thickening. Shimmer, width.
- Flanger: very short modulated delay, comb filtering. Jet-like sweep.
- Phaser: cascaded all-pass filters create swept notch pattern. Swooshing, less extreme than flanging.
- Tremolo: amplitude modulation. Pulsing volume. Vintage amp effect.
- Delay: echo repeats. Slapback (short, single), tape (warm degrading), digital (clean), analog (dark, filtered).
- Reverb: spring (guitar-specific, splashy), plate, hall, shimmer.

## Bass Guitar

### Construction & Tone

- 4-string standard tuning: E1-A1-D2-G2. 5-string adds low B0. 6-string adds high C3.
- Scale length: 34" standard (Fender), 35" for 5/6-string (better low B tension), 30" short scale (warmer, easier to play).
- Pickups: Jazz Bass (two single-coils, versatile, clear), Precision Bass (split-coil humbucker, punchy midrange, Motown/rock standard), Music Man (large humbucker, aggressive, clear), active preamp (onboard EQ, modern extended range).

### Techniques

- Fingerstyle: index and middle alternation. Warm, round. Standard approach.
- Slap: thumb strikes string against fretboard (percussive thump) + finger pops (bright snap). Funk essential. Larry Graham, Marcus Miller, Flea.
- Pick: brighter, more attack. Punk, rock, metal, Motown (Carol Kaye).
- Tapping: two-hand fretboard technique. Extended range. Victor Wooten, Billy Sheehan.
- Ghost notes: muted percussive hits between pitched notes. Defines groove feel. Essential for funk, R&B.
- Harmonics: natural (bell-like, Jaco Pastorius "Portrait of Tracy" = landmark harmonic piece) and artificial.
- Palm muting: muted, short notes. Foam under strings at bridge = Motown damped sound. James Jamerson.
- Double thumbing: alternating thumb down/up on same string. Modern technique. Victor Wooten.
- Chords: possible but muddy in low register. More effective with higher voicings, especially on 6-string.

### Role in Arrangement
- Harmonic foundation: defines chord root, grounds harmonic structure.
- Rhythmic anchor: locks with kick drum. The kick-bass relationship defines genre feel.
- Melodic bass: countermelody, walking bass (jazz), fills. Bass as lead voice.
- Frequency range: low B = 31Hz, low E = 41Hz. Useful harmonics and attack transients extend to ~5kHz.

## Keyboards & Synthesizers

### Piano (Extended)

**Prepared piano** (John Cage, Sonatas and Interludes):
- Objects placed on/between strings transform piano into tuned percussion ensemble.
- Bolts/screws: metallic, gamelan-like.
- Rubber/erasers: dampened, muted, clavichord-like.
- Felt: soft, reduced sustain, pizzicato-like.
- Each preparation unique per note. Creating a prepared piano = composing the instrument itself.

**Extended techniques**:
- String plucking (inside piano): harp-like quality, exposed strings.
- String strumming: glissando directly on strings.
- Harmonics: touch nodal point on string while striking key.
- Muted notes: hand on strings while playing = staccato, clavichord effect.
- Cluster playing: forearm or fist on keys. Henry Cowell pioneered forearm clusters.
- Sostenuto pedal selective sustain: sustain specific notes while playing others normally. Compositional tool for resonance layers.

### Organ

**Pipe organ**:
- Registration: stop combinations create timbre. Principal (foundation, clear), Flute (round, soft), Reed (bright, buzzy), String (thin, keen), Mixture (adds upper harmonics for brilliance).
- Multiple manuals: Great, Swell, Choir, Solo, Pedal. Each division has independent registration.
- Enormous range: pedal division extends below piano range. Full organ = massive dynamic range.
- Spatial: pipes physically distributed throughout building. Inherently spatial instrument.

**Hammond organ** (tonewheel):
- Drawbars: 9 harmonic levels (sub-fundamental, fundamental, 2nd through 8th harmonics). Additive synthesis via analog tonewheels.
- Percussion: brief attack transient (2nd or 3rd harmonic). Adds definition to note onset.
- Key click: mechanical contact noise. Originally a defect, now desirable character.
- Leslie speaker: rotating speaker creates Doppler-based modulation. Slow rotation = gentle chorus. Fast = intense tremolo/vibrato. THE Hammond sound. The physical interaction between speaker and room = unreproducible by effects alone (though simulations are close).
- Overdrive: pushing preamp creates warm, growling distortion. Blues/rock organ sound.

**Electric piano**:
- Rhodes: struck tine + tone bar. Bell-like, warm, "glassy." Velocity-sensitive: soft = pure, mellow; hard = bright, barking. Tremolo and chorus are standard effects. Herbie Hancock, Stevie Wonder, Radiohead.
- Wurlitzer: struck reed. More aggressive, reedy, nasal. Naturally overdrives at high velocity. Ray Charles, Supertramp.

### Synthesizer Performance

Beyond sound generation (covered in SYNTHESIS_SKILL.md), synthesizers have specific performance characteristics:
- Velocity sensitivity: harder = louder/brighter. Depth of velocity->filter mapping defines expressiveness.
- Aftertouch: pressure after key-down. Channel aftertouch (global) vs polyphonic aftertouch (per-note). Maps to vibrato, filter, effects. MPE (MIDI Polyphonic Expression): per-note pitch bend, slide, pressure.
- Mod wheel: left-hand real-time control. Standard mapping: vibrato depth or filter cutoff.
- Pitch bend: spring-loaded wheel for real-time pitch bending. Range typically set to +/- 2 semitones but configurable (full octave for slides, quarter-tone for microtonal).
- Keyboard tracking: filter cutoff following pitch. Higher notes = brighter. Essential for natural-sounding patches.

## Vocals

### Voice Types & Ranges

**Female**:
- Soprano: C4-C6 (coloratura extends to F6+). Bright, pure, penetrating. Dramatic soprano: heavier, more powerful. Lyric soprano: lighter, more agile.
- Mezzo-soprano: A3-A5. Warmer, richer than soprano. Operatic standard female range.
- Contralto: F3-F5. Deep, dark, rare true contralto. Rich low register.

**Male**:
- Tenor: C3-C5 (some to D5+). Bright, heroic, projecting. Lyric tenor: lighter. Dramatic tenor: heavier.
- Baritone: A2-A4. Most common male voice. Warm, versatile, substantial.
- Bass: E2-E4. Deep, resonant, powerful foundation.
- Countertenor: male singing in falsetto/head voice range, C3-C6. Early music, contemporary. Alfred Deller, Andreas Scholl.

### Register Transitions

- Chest voice: lower register, full vocal fold vibration. Powerful, resonant.
- Head voice: upper register, partial fold vibration. Lighter, more resonant in head cavities.
- Mixed voice (voix mixte): blended chest/head. Smooth transition, belt technique. Contemporary vocal pedagogy focuses on eliminating the "break" through mixing.
- Falsetto: highest male register, edges of folds only. Breathy, lighter. Distinct from trained countertenor head voice.
- Vocal fry: lowest register, irregular fold vibration. Creaky, used in speech and contemporary vocal styles.
- Whistle register: highest possible (above ~C6). Mariah Carey, Ariana Grande.
- Passaggio: transition point between registers. Training smooths this. Untrained voices "break" audibly.

### Extended Vocal Techniques

- Overtone singing (throat singing): manipulate vocal tract resonances to amplify specific overtones, creating perception of two simultaneous pitches. Tuvan: khoomei (throat), sygyt (whistling overtone), kargyraa (sub-harmonic drone). Mongolian, Sardinian traditions.
- Vocal multiphonics: singing while humming/growling simultaneously produces multiple pitches. Different from overtone singing: two independent sound sources rather than filtering one.
- Sprechgesang/Sprechstimme: half-sung, half-spoken. Approximate pitch indicated but not sustained. Schoenberg (Pierrot Lunaire). Berg (Wozzeck). Notated with X on note stems.
- Beatboxing: vocal percussion, vocal bass, simultaneous singing + percussion. Extended technique tradition within hip-hop culture.
- Growl/distortion: throat constriction creates rough, distorted tone. Metal, blues. False cord vs fry scream techniques.
- Extended phonemes: sustained consonants (m, n, l as pitched sustain), clicks, hisses, plosives as percussive events, breath sounds.
- Circular breathing: continuous sound without breath break. Possible but extremely rare in singing. Standard in didgeridoo, some wind playing.

### Vocal Production

- Proximity effect: closer to microphone = more bass boost (directional mics only). Intimacy vs boomy. Manage with distance and HPF.
- Double tracking: record same part twice, pan L/R. Natural width from micro-timing/pitch differences. ADT (Artificial Double Tracking): short modulated delay simulates doubling.
- Pitch correction: Auto-Tune (real-time; transparent correction or deliberate "Auto-Tune effect" at fast speed setting), Melodyne (offline, surgical, can edit individual notes within chords). Both: tools for correction AND creative effect.
- Vocal comping: record multiple takes, select best phrases from each. Standard studio workflow.
- De-essing: reduce sibilance (s, sh, ch) in 4-8kHz. Dedicated de-esser or dynamic EQ. Essential for most vocal recordings.
- Vocal layering: harmonies, octave doubles, unison stacks. Each layer adds density and perceived width.

## World & Folk Instruments (Brief Reference)

### Strings
- **Sitar**: sympathetic strings create shimmering resonance. Movable frets allow microtonal bending (meend). Indian classical.
- **Oud**: fretless lute. Continuous pitch capability. Middle Eastern/Arabic. Maqam system.
- **Koto**: Japanese 13-string zither. Bridges movable for tuning. Plucked with picks (tsume).
- **Erhu**: Chinese 2-string bowed instrument. No fingerboard (strings pressed, not fretted). Extremely expressive, vocal quality.
- **Banjo**: bright, percussive attack. 5-string (bluegrass, clawhammer), 4-string (tenor, Dixieland). Membrane head = distinctive ring.
- **Mandolin**: paired strings (courses), tremolo technique fundamental. Bright, cutting.
- **Bouzouki**: Greek (3/4 course) or Irish (4 course). Bright, resonant, modal.

### Wind
- **Shakuhachi**: Japanese bamboo flute. Extremely breathy, wide dynamic and timbral range. Pitch bending via embouchure and head angle. Zen meditation instrument.
- **Duduk**: Armenian double-reed. Deeply mournful, vocal quality. Drone accompaniment standard.
- **Didgeridoo**: Australian Aboriginal. Continuous drone via circular breathing. Overtone manipulation, vocal effects while playing.
- **Bagpipes**: multiple reeds. Continuous sound via bag reservoir. Drones (sustained pitches) + chanter (melody). Scottish, Irish, Eastern European, Middle Eastern varieties.

### Percussion
- **Tabla**: Indian paired drums (tabla = treble, bayan = bass). Extraordinarily expressive. Spoken rhythm syllables (bols) define strokes. Tonal range from pitched to unpitched.
- **Djembe**: West African hand drum. Three basic tones: bass (center), tone (edge), slap (edge, sharp). Dynamic range from whisper to thunderous.
- **Cajon**: box drum, Peruvian origin. Seated playing. Snare wires optional. Acoustic drum kit substitute.
- **Gamelan**: Indonesian tuned percussion ensemble. Metallophones, gongs, drums. Non-Western tuning systems (slendro: 5-tone, pelog: 7-tone). Influenced Debussy, Britten, Reich.
- **Steel pan**: Caribbean. Melodic pitched percussion from oil drums. Chromatic range. Bright, shimmering, sustained.

### Keyboards
- **Harmonium**: bellows-driven reed organ. Pedal or hand-pumped. Indian classical accompaniment standard. Continuous dynamic control via bellows pressure.
- **Accordion**: bellows + reeds + keyboard/buttons. Right hand: melody. Left hand: bass/chords. Bellows = expression/dynamics. French musette, tango, folk worldwide.

Each of these instruments represents centuries of performance tradition, unique tuning systems, and timbral characteristics that can inform contemporary composition and production.
