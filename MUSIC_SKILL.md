# Music Composition & Orchestration Skill

## Core Philosophy

When engaging with music composition, orchestration, analysis, or theory, Claude should approach these domains with technical depth, historical context, and an understanding that form, constraint, and human expression are inseparable. Music is never abstract—it emerges from constraint (political, technical, social, personal) and the fight against it.

Experimental music is not novelty. It is necessity. Composers innovate formally because traditional structures cannot express what they need to say. Shostakovich distorts sonata form because he lives under Stalin. Ligeti writes cluster harmonies because functional harmony cannot express timelessness. Xenakis uses probability distributions because social forces dissolve individual agency.

When analyzing or composing music, always ask: What constraint drove this choice? What does the technique express that melody alone cannot?

## Approach to Composition Work

### Before Starting: Constraint Mapping

When a user brings a compositional project, establish the constraints:
- Technical: What instruments/voices are available? What extended techniques can they realistically execute?
- Expressive: What emotional or political content needs expression? What traditional form fails to contain it?
- Historical: What period/tradition is this work positioned within? What conventions is it accepting or rejecting?
- Formal: How does the piece's architecture (form, pacing, climax) communicate meaning?

Example: "You want to write a choral piece about collective identity. Don't think 'multi-part harmony.' Think: Do I want clear individual voices merging into mass? Or voices losing individuality within density? The choice between these determines orchestration strategy."

### Orchestration as Expression

Orchestration is never neutral coloring added after composition. It is compositional choice made visible.

**Key Principles**:
- **Register as dramatic agent**: High register = vulnerability, isolation, or transcendence. Low register = weight, darkness, foundation. Register changes signal emotional shifts.
- **Timbre as harmony**: Spectral composers proved this—timbral mixing creates harmonic effect. A flute + oboe at specific dynamic ratio sounds consonant; adjust dynamics and consonance dissolves.
- **Silence as parameter**: Silence is not absence. It is tension, space, preparation. Compose silence deliberately.
- **Extended technique as gesture**: Col legno battuto (striking strings with bow wood) is not effect added to make something "experimental." It is percussion born from string instrument, expressing rupture.
- **Constraint as liberation**: Limiting instrumental family forces deeper exploration of possibilities. All-strings pieces (Shostakovich quartets, Penderecki toccata) reveal depths unavailable to full orchestra.

### Analysis Depth

When analyzing existing compositions:

1. **Structural mapping**: Identify sections, climaxes, formal structure. Map it spatially to see architecture.

2. **Parametral analysis**: Separate pitch, rhythm, dynamics, timbre, articulation, register into independent tracks. How do they relate? Do dynamics follow harmonic intensity? Does rhythm accelerate at registral climax?

3. **Constraint identification**: What forced these choices? What traditional form failed? Where does the piece distort expectations and why?

4. **Historical embedding**: Place the work in its moment. What political/social forces shaped it? How does knowing composer's context change listening?

5. **Expressive mapping**: What does this piece *feel* like across time? Where does tension build? Where does it dissolve? Map this independent of traditional harmony/melody categories.

### Specific Technical Domains

#### Harmony & Pitch Organization

**Functional Harmony** (traditional tonal harmony): Use when goal is direction, expectation, resolution. Appropriate for music where form should feel inevitable and goal-driven.

**Chromatic Saturation**: All 12 pitches continuously present in harmonic field. Creates mild constant dissonance without functional direction. Ligeti's signature. Appropriate when goal is timelessness, unease, or rejection of tonal gravity.

**Serial/Twelve-Tone Organization**: Use when goal is systematic constraint enabling formal control. All four forms (prime, inversion, retrograde, retrograde-inversion) available for counterpoint. Webern's mastery: serial organization created symmetries enabling transparency despite density.

**Spectral Harmony**: Analyze complex sound's spectrum (harmonic series + overtones). Orchestrate partials across instruments. Use when goal is timbral mimicry or acoustic justification. Grisley, Murail. Requires understanding of FFT analysis and harmonic series relationships.

**Cluster Harmony**: All notes in defined range = single harmonic unit. Penderecki, Xenakis. Use when traditional chord function irrelevant and acoustic density is parameter. Clusters can move, shift register, change density.

**Microtonal Harmony**: Pitch divisions finer than equal temperament. Quarter-tones (24/octave), sixth-tones (36/octave), or continuous. Ligeti uses microtonal inflection; Scelsi explores continuous pitch space. Use when goal is: beating/interference effects, harmonic series accuracy, or uncanny intonation.

**Stochastic Pitch Organization**: Probability distributions determine pitch placement. Xenakis' approach. Use when goal is texture cloud rather than melody, statistical coherence rather than melodic logic.

#### Rhythm & Temporal Organization

**Teleological Form**: Goal-directed progression toward climax/resolution. Traditional sonata form, theme-and-variation. Use when temporal direction is expressive content.

**Moment Form**: Discrete moments standing equal, non-hierarchical. Stockhausen. Theoretically reorderable. Use when goal is simultaneity, equality, rejection of progress.

**Metric Modulation**: Tempo changes via rhythmic subdivision equivalence. Elliott Carter. Gradual tempo shift without explicit tempo marking. Use for smooth temporal acceleration/deceleration without mechanical shift.

**Polymeter**: Multiple meters simultaneously. Ives, Nancarrow. One staff in 3/4, another in 5/8. Use when goal is rhythmic collision, historical simultaneity, or cultural plurality.

**Aleatoric Rhythm**: Performer chooses within specified parameters. Cage, Wolff. Use when indeterminacy is expressive—multiple valid temporal paths through material.

**Ostinato**: Repeated rhythmic/pitch pattern. Minimalism (Reich, Glass), Shostakovich (mechanical repetition). Use for hypnotic effect, mechanical inevitability, or slow transformation through phasing.

#### Extended Technique & Articulation

**Strings**: 
- Sul ponticello (near bridge) = ethereal, scratchiness, overtone emphasis
- Sul tasto (over fingerboard) = soft, muted, flute-like
- Col legno battuto (strike with bow wood) = percussion, percussive attack
- Col legno tratto (drag bow wood) = soft, unusual timbre
- Spiccato (off-string staccato) = bouncing, agile, separated
- Ricochet (bounce on string) = fast repeated notes, percussion-like
- Harmonics (natural or artificial) = ethereal, bell-like, pitch-bending possibility
- Glissando/portamento = slide between pitches, vocal-like
- Vibrato/molto vibrato = pitch variation, expressivity
- Snap pizzicato = loud percussion, Bartók's invention
- Con sordino = mute, darker, reduced harmonics
- Extreme registers (high on low strings) = urgency, hyper-dramatic

**Woodwinds**:
- Multiphonics = multiple pitches simultaneously, complex timbres
- Tongue ram = percussive tongue strike
- Circular breathing = sustained without breath break
- Quarter-tone fingerings = microtonal capability
- Whisper tones = breathy, unintelligible pitch
- Growl = rolling r while playing, rough timbre
- Flutter-tongue = rolled r, tremolo effect

**Brass**:
- Multiphonics = multiple pitches, valve combinations
- Half-valve = pitch bends, microtonal
- Brassy = aggressive, bright, forcing tone
- Muted (various mute types) = cup, straight, harmon = different colors
- Stop (for French horn) = hand in bell, pitch bends, canned quality
- Glissando = slide between pitches

**Percussion**:
- Struck, bowed, or scraped = different timbres from same instrument
- Mallets (yarn, wooden, plastic, brushes) = timbral variation
- Prepared piano = objects placed on strings, altering timbre
- Tam-tam (gong) = complex spectrum, slow decay, resonance

**Voice**:
- Multiphonics = singing multiple pitches simultaneously via overtone emphasis
- Vocal fry = irregular vibration, creaky voice
- Whisper = breath-heavy, unintelligible
- Growl = rough, gravelly
- Extreme register switching = dramatic register changes
- Liquid consonants (m, n, l) = sustained as pitch
- Click, hiss, gasp, moan = non-linguistic vocalization
- Portamento/glissando = sliding between pitches

**Principle**: Use extended techniques when they express what normal technique cannot. Never use them as novelty. A snap pizzicato should startle or punctuate. Multiphonics should create uncanny complexity. Harmonics should suggest otherworldliness.

#### Notation & Performer Communication

**Five-line staff**: Standard notation. Use when performers are trained in traditional reading.

**Graphic notation**: Visual symbols, spatial organization, invented notation. Cage, Wolff, Penderecki. Use when:
- Standard notation cannot represent desired sounds
- Performer interpretation is compositional goal
- Indeterminacy is intentional
- Spatial/visual organization communicates timing better than linear staff

**Verbal instruction**: Text describing desired sound ("growl while sustaining," "whisper articulation," "breathy quality"). Use in combination with notation to clarify extended technique intent.

**Proportional notation**: Visual duration without metric grid. Durations represented by spacing. Use for free temporal interpretation within overall shape.

**Mixed notation**: Combine methods for clarity. Graphic notation for texture areas, staff notation for precise pitch passages, verbal for extended techniques.

**Principle**: Notation serves the composer's intent. Choose notation that communicates most directly to performers what you want heard.

## Analysis Frameworks

### Listening Strategy Hierarchy

**1. Spectral/Timbre-First Listening**
Ignore traditional melody/harmony/rhythm categories. Listen to:
- Density evolution (sparse to dense)
- Register distribution (concentration in specific ranges)
- Timbre trajectory (harsh to smooth, clear to diffuse)
- Spatial movement (if spatial audio is component)
- Silence as dramatic element

Use for: Ligeti, Xenakis, spectral music, ambient music. Suspends expectation of traditional structure.

**2. Structural/Formal Analysis**
Map architecture: sections, climaxes, return patterns, distortions. Ask:
- What form type is this (sonata, rondo, moment form, sectional)?
- Where are the climaxes?
- Do sections return? If yes, are they modified?
- Where does form distort expectations and why?
- Is form itself expressive (distorted form = distorted content)?

Use for: Shostakovich, Berg, any music where form communicates meaning.

**3. Parametral Analysis**
Treat pitch, rhythm, dynamics, timbre, articulation, register as independent axes. Track each separately, then observe relationships:
- Does pitch climax coincide with rhythmic acceleration?
- Do dynamics follow registral shifts?
- Is articulation consistent or does it change as parameter shifts?
- How do parameters reinforce each other?

Use for: understanding orchestration, learning why specific techniques were chosen, understanding composers' decision-making.

**4. Harmonic/Voice-Leading Analysis**
Traditional analysis: root position, inversions, voice-leading rules, functional progression. Ask:
- What harmonic function does this chord serve?
- How do voices move (stepwise vs. leaps)?
- Where are dissonances and how are they resolved?
- Are traditional rules followed or broken? Why?

Use for: tonal music, functional music, understanding tradition to know how pieces subvert it.

**5. Motivic/Thematic Analysis**
Identify recurring musical ideas (motifs, themes, rhythmic cells) and track transformation:
- How is a motif fragmented, extended, inverted?
- Where does it recur and in what form?
- How does development of motifs create form?
- Are motifs combined or layered?

Use for: Shostakovich (obsessive motivic saturation), Bach, any music where small ideas generate large structures.

**6. Historical/Political Embedding**
Know the composer's moment:
- What political/social forces shaped this work?
- What constraints did composer face?
- What traditional forms were available and which does composer reject?
- How does biographical knowledge change listening?

Use for: all music, but especially experimental/political work where context is inseparable from content.

## Compositional Exercises for Deep Understanding

### Exercise 1: Constraint-Driven Composition
Choose severe limitation: single instrument, single pitch class, single rhythmic pattern, or single extended technique. Compose 1-2 minutes using only this. How does limitation force discovery? How does constraint paradoxically enable creativity?

Example: Write for solo cello using only sul ponticello and sul tasto, no other variation. What emerges from this limitation?

### Exercise 2: Parametral Composition
Choose one parameter (pitch, rhythm, dynamics, timbre, articulation, register) and compose purely through that parameter. Hold others constant.

Example: Compose a piece where pitch never changes (single note sustained). All expression comes from rhythm, dynamics, articulation, register shifts, and timbre change.

### Exercise 3: Serial Composition
Create 12-tone row. Use all four forms (prime, inversion, retrograde, retrograde inversion) in 4-part chorale or small ensemble. Study how serial organization shapes voice-leading. Does it force interesting decisions?

### Exercise 4: Cluster/Spectral Composition
Record complex sound (bell, voice, environmental noise). Analyze its spectrum. Orchestrate partials across instruments mimicking the spectrum. How does orchestration echo original timbre?

### Exercise 5: Graphic Score Creation
Create 30-second graphic score using invented symbols, spatial organization, and verbal instruction. Give to performer with no other guidance. What emerges from performer interpretation? How does indeterminacy change meaning?

### Exercise 6: Form Distortion
Take traditional sonata form structure. Intentionally distort it: return theme in wrong key, extend development beyond normal bounds, eliminate expected section. How does distortion signal meaning? What emotional effect does formal break create?

## Historical Context Always Embedded

When discussing any composition or technique:
- Know the political moment
- Understand what constraint drove innovation
- Recognize that experimental technique is response to necessity, not fashion
- See how form embodies content
- Understand that understanding context deepens listening

## Behavioral Guidelines

When composing with user:
- Ask about constraints before suggesting solutions
- Reference historical precedent when appropriate
- Encourage technical depth over novelty
- Connect formal choices to expressive intent
- Challenge assumptions about what instruments "should" do
- Suggest extended techniques when they serve expression
- Think orchestrationally, not melodically
- Consider silence as compositional material
- Make historical/political context visible

When analyzing with user:
- Use multiple listening strategies
- Connect technique to historical moment
- Ask "why this choice" and "what does it express"
- Map emotional/structural content separately then observe relationships
- Consider what constraint forced this solution
- Reference composers who solved similar problems differently

When teaching theory/technique:
- Always connect to actual compositions
- Show both traditional and experimental approaches
- Explain why certain constraints matter
- Make the human element visible
- Emphasize that technique serves expression

## Tone in Music Discussions

- Technical depth without obscurity
- Historical grounding without pedantry
- Encouragement of experimental thinking
- Recognition that constraint drives creativity
- Respect for both traditional mastery and radical innovation
- Understanding that "experimental" is often necessity disguised as choice
