# Music Terminology: Comprehensive Reference

## Tempo Markings (Slowest to Fastest)

Primary tempo terms indicating speed and often character. BPM ranges are approximate and historically fluid; different eras and editors interpret ranges differently.

### Core Tempo Scale

**Larghissimo**: extremely slow, broadest possible tempo. <25 BPM. Rarely encountered outside specific dramatic contexts.

**Grave**: very slow, solemn, heavy. 25-45 BPM. Implies weight and gravity beyond mere slowness. Beethoven Pathetique sonata introduction.

**Largo**: broadly, very slow. 40-60 BPM. Spacious, dignified. Handel "Largo" (actually marked larghetto). One of the most misused tempo terms.

**Lento**: slowly. 45-60 BPM. Simply slow without the weight/gravity connotation of grave or the breadth of largo.

**Larghetto**: somewhat broadly, slightly faster than largo. 60-66 BPM. Diminutive of largo. Still slow but with more motion.

**Adagio**: at ease, slowly. 66-76 BPM. One of the most common slow markings. Implies leisurely, comfortable pace. Adagio movements are the slow heart of classical works.

**Adagietto**: slightly faster than adagio. 72-76 BPM. Diminutive. Mahler Symphony 5, fourth movement.

**Andante**: walking pace. 76-108 BPM. Moderate-slow. Literally "going" or "walking." One of the most common tempo markings. Not truly slow - implies gentle forward motion.

**Andantino**: slightly faster than andante. 80-108 BPM. Historically ambiguous - some interpret as slower than andante. Modern convention: slightly faster.

**Marcia moderato**: moderate march tempo. 83-85 BPM. Military march association.

**Andante moderato**: between andante and moderato. 92-112 BPM. Moderate walking pace.

**Moderato**: moderately. 108-120 BPM. The neutral center of the tempo spectrum. Neither fast nor slow.

**Allegretto**: moderately fast, lighter than allegro. 112-120 BPM. Implies lightness and grace rather than just speed. Diminutive of allegro.

**Allegro moderato**: close to but not quite allegro. 116-120 BPM. Restrained allegro energy.

**Allegro**: fast, lively, bright. 120-156 BPM. The most common fast tempo marking. Literally "cheerful/merry." Standard first-movement tempo in classical sonata form.

**Vivace**: lively, fast. 156-176 BPM. More energetic than allegro. Implies vivacity and spirit beyond pure speed.

**Vivacissimo**: very fast and lively. 172-176 BPM. Superlative of vivace.

**Allegrissimo**: very fast. 172-176 BPM. Superlative of allegro. Rare.

**Presto**: very fast. 168-200 BPM. Implies urgency, brilliance, virtuosic energy. Standard finale tempo.

**Prestissimo**: as fast as possible. 200+ BPM. Extreme speed. Beethoven used this in late sonatas for passages of near-impossibility.

## Tempo Modifiers

Italian modifiers that qualify tempo markings. Combined with base tempo: e.g., "Allegro ma non troppo" = fast but not too much.

**Molto**: very, much. Molto allegro = very fast. Molto adagio = very slow.
**Assai**: very, enough. Allegro assai = very fast (historically could mean "fast enough" rather than "very fast").
**Poco**: a little. Poco allegro = a little fast. Poco a poco = little by little (gradual change).
**Piu**: more. Piu mosso = more motion (faster). Piu lento = slower.
**Meno**: less. Meno mosso = less motion (slower). Meno forte = less loud.
**Non troppo**: not too much. Allegro non troppo = fast but not too fast. Brahms favorite qualifier.
**Non tanto**: not so much. Similar to non troppo but slightly different nuance.
**Con moto**: with motion. Andante con moto = walking pace with forward momentum. Implies not dragging.
**Con brio**: with spirit/vigor. Allegro con brio = fast with brilliance. Beethoven Symphony 5 opening.
**Con fuoco**: with fire. Implies passionate intensity at whatever tempo.
**Ma**: but. Used as conjunction between directives. Allegro ma non troppo = fast but not too much.
**Quasi**: almost, as if. Quasi adagio = almost adagio. Andante quasi allegretto = walking pace almost as if allegretto.
**Sempre**: always, throughout. Sempre forte = loud throughout. Sempre accelerando = continuously getting faster.
**Subito**: suddenly. Subito piano = suddenly soft. Subito forte = suddenly loud.
**Ben**: well. Ben marcato = well marked/accented.
**Mosso**: motion/movement. Used with piu/meno. Piu mosso = faster (more motion). Meno mosso = slower (less motion).

## Tempo Change Terms

Terms indicating tempo transitions during a passage.

### Gradual Acceleration
**Accelerando** (accel.): gradually getting faster. The standard term for speeding up.
**Stringendo**: pressing forward, tightening. Implies urgency, increasing tension alongside acceleration.
**Stretto**: tight, compressed. In fugue: entries overlapping. As tempo direction: accelerating, tightening. Context determines meaning.
**Incalzando**: pursuing, pressing. Similar to stringendo with more urgency.
**Affrettando**: hurrying. Slightly more casual acceleration.

### Gradual Deceleration
**Ritardando** (rit., ritard.): gradually slowing down. The most common deceleration term.
**Rallentando** (rall.): gradually slowing. Practically interchangeable with ritardando in modern usage. Some traditions distinguish: rallentando = broadening (implying louder), ritardando = simply slowing.
**Allargando**: broadening, widening. Slowing with growing weight and fullness. Often implies crescendo alongside the slowing.
**Ritenuto** (riten.): held back, immediately slower. Unlike ritardando (gradual), ritenuto implies a more sudden reduction in tempo.
**Slentando**: slowing down. Less common.
**Calando**: decreasing. Both tempo and volume diminish together. Dying away.
**Morendo**: dying. Gradual decrease in both tempo and dynamics to nothing. Implies extinction.
**Smorzando** (smorz.): extinguishing, dying away. Similar to morendo.
**Perdendosi**: losing itself. Gradually fading to nothing in both dynamics and tempo.

### Tempo Restoration & Relationship
**A tempo**: return to the previous tempo after a deviation (ritardando, accelerando, etc.).
**Tempo primo** (Tempo I): return to the tempo of the opening. Used after extended tempo changes.
**L'istesso tempo**: the same tempo. When meter changes but beat speed stays constant. E.g., quarter note in 4/4 = dotted quarter in 6/8.
**Doppio movimento**: double the speed. Tempo doubles.
**Doppio piu lento**: half the speed. Tempo halves.

### Flexible Tempo
**Tempo rubato** (rubato): "stolen time." Flexible tempo where some beats are stretched and others compressed, maintaining overall pulse across a phrase. Not merely "slowing down" - time borrowed from one beat is repaid on another. Chopin's signature technique.
**A piacere**: at pleasure. Performer decides tempo freely.
**Ad libitum** (ad lib.): at liberty. Free tempo and/or improvised content.
**Senza tempo**: without tempo. No regular beat. Free rhythm.
**Colla voce** (colla parte): with the voice (with the soloist). Accompaniment follows soloist's tempo fluctuations.

## Dynamic Markings

### Absolute Dynamic Levels

From softest to loudest. These are relative, not absolute volume levels. ppp in one context may be louder than pp in another.

**pppp**: as soft as physically possible. Extremely rare. Tcherepnin, some Mahler.
**ppp** (pianississimo): extremely soft. Barely audible. Requires exceptional control.
**pp** (pianissimo): very soft. Standard soft marking for delicate passages.
**p** (piano): soft. The default "quiet" marking.
**mp** (mezzo piano): moderately soft. The quieter side of moderate.
**mf** (mezzo forte): moderately loud. The louder side of moderate. The "default" dynamic of most ensemble playing.
**f** (forte): loud. The standard "loud" marking.
**ff** (fortissimo): very loud. Full power.
**fff** (fortississimo): extremely loud. Maximum orchestral force.
**ffff**: as loud as physically possible. Rare. Tcherepnin.

### Sudden Dynamic Changes
**Sforzando** (sfz): sudden strong accent on a single note/chord. An explosive emphasis that may occur at any dynamic level. sfz within piano = sudden accent returning to piano.
**Sforzato** (sf): similar to sforzando. Some editions distinguish: sf = slightly less forceful than sfz. In practice, interchangeable.
**Forzando** (fz): forced, accented. Similar to sforzando.
**Fortepiano** (fp): loud then immediately soft. Single note/chord attacked forte, immediately dropping to piano.
**Sforzando-piano** (sfzp): sforzando accent followed immediately by piano. Explosive attack, immediate retreat.
**Rinforzando** (rfz, rf): reinforcing. Sudden increase in volume, often on a single note or short phrase. Less explosive than sforzando.
**Forte-piano-crescendo** (fp cresc.): loud attack, drop to soft, then gradually build back.

### Gradual Dynamic Changes
**Crescendo** (cresc.): gradually getting louder. Indicated by term or hairpin (<).
**Decrescendo** (decresc.): gradually getting softer. Indicated by term or hairpin (>).
**Diminuendo** (dim.): gradually getting softer. Interchangeable with decrescendo in modern usage.

### Special Dynamic Terms
**Al niente**: to nothing. Diminuendo to silence. Often written with closing hairpin ending in "n" or "o."
**Dal niente**: from nothing. Crescendo from silence.
**Sotto voce**: under the voice. Very soft, subdued, as if whispering. Implies restrained quality beyond mere quietness.
**Mezza voce**: half voice. Restrained, moderate volume. Implies holding back deliberately.
**A voce piena**: at full voice. Unrestricted volume.
**Perdendo/perdendosi**: losing itself. Dying away to nothing. Dynamics and often tempo both decrease.
**Calando**: decreasing. Diminishing in both volume and tempo.
**Morendo**: dying. Fading to silence.
**Smorzando** (smorz.): extinguishing. Fading to nothing.
**Marcato**: marked. Each note emphasized. Not necessarily louder overall, but with deliberate weight on each note.

Terms indicating mood, character, and interpretive approach. These shape how notes are played beyond pitch, rhythm, and dynamics.

**Amabile**: amiable, sweet. Gentle, pleasant character.
**Amoroso**: lovingly, tenderly.
**Animato**: animated, lively. With life and energy.
**Appassionato**: passionately. With intense emotional commitment. Beethoven Sonata Op. 57.
**Brioso**: with vigor, spirited.
**Cantabile**: in a singing style. Lyrical, vocal-like phrasing. One of the most common expression markings.
**Capriccioso**: capricious, whimsical. Free, unpredictable character.
**Con affetto**: with feeling/affection.
**Con amore**: with love.
**Con anima**: with soul/spirit.
**Con brio**: with spirit, vigor. Brightness and energy.
**Con calore**: with warmth.
**Con dolore**: with sorrow, grief.
**Con espressione**: with expression. General instruction to play expressively.
**Con fuoco**: with fire. Passionate, intense.
**Con grazia**: with grace. Elegant, refined.
**Con moto**: with motion. Forward momentum, not dragging.
**Con passione**: with passion.
**Con sentimento**: with feeling.
**Con spirito**: with spirit. Lively, energetic.
**Con tenerezza**: with tenderness. Gentle, delicate.
**Dolce**: sweet, gentle. Soft and lyrical. Very common.
**Dolcissimo**: very sweet. Superlative of dolce.
**Doloroso**: sorrowful, painful.
**Drammatico**: dramatic. With theatrical intensity.
**Elegiaco**: elegiac. Mournful, lamenting.
**Energico**: energetic, vigorous. With force and vitality.
**Eroico**: heroic. Grand, noble character.
**Espressivo** (espress., espr.): expressively. Play with emotional depth and personal interpretation.
**Feroce**: fierce, ferocious. Wild, aggressive.
**Fieramente**: fiercely, proudly.
**Furioso**: furiously. Extremely agitated, violent character.
**Giocoso**: playful, humorous. Light, game-like.
**Gioioso**: joyful, jubilant.
**Grandioso**: grandly, magnificently. Broad, imposing.
**Grazioso**: graceful, elegant.
**Impetuoso**: impetuous, rushing forward with force.
**Lacrimoso**: tearful, mournful.
**Lamentoso**: lamenting, mournful.
**Leggiero** (legg.): light, nimble. Delicate touch, airy quality.
**Lugubre**: lugubrious, gloomy, dark.

**Misterioso**: mysteriously.

**Spiritoso**: spirited, witty.
**Strepitoso**: noisy, boisterous. With commotion.
**Tenero**: tender, delicate.
**Tranquillo**: tranquil, calm, peaceful.
**Trionfale**: triumphant.
**Vigoroso**: vigorous, strong.

## Articulation Terms

How individual notes are attacked, sustained, and released.

**Legato**: smooth, connected. No silence between notes. Notes flow into one another. Bowing: multiple notes per bow stroke. Keyboard: fingers overlap slightly. The default for lyrical passages.
**Legatissimo**: as smooth/connected as possible. Extreme legato.
**Portato** (mezzo-staccato, louré): slightly separated but not fully detached. Gentle articulation within a slur. Notated: dots under a slur. Between legato and staccato.
**Non legato**: not connected but not staccato. Each note slightly separated. Neutral articulation.
**Staccato**: short, detached. Note significantly shorter than written value. Notated: dot above/below note. Typically half the written value or less.
**Staccatissimo**: extremely short, detached. The shortest possible note. Notated: triangle/wedge above note. Barely a point of sound.
**Tenuto** (ten.): held. Note sustained for its full written value (or slightly beyond). Notated: horizontal line above note. Implies slight emphasis without accent.
**Marcato** (marc.): marked, accented. Each note distinctly emphasized. Notated: open wedge (^) above note. Stronger than accent (>), implying both attack and sustained weight.
**Accent** (>): emphasized attack. Stronger onset than surrounding notes. Less weight than marcato.
**Martellato**: hammered. Very strong, percussive staccato. Each note struck with force. String technique: heavy on-string staccato.
**Pesante**: heavy. Each note weighted. Often implies slight tenuto quality with deliberate placement.
**Portamento**: carrying the tone. Smooth glide from one pitch to another. Different from glissando: portamento slides through continuous pitch space, glissando may imply passing through intermediate scale tones. Vocal and string technique primarily.
**Sforzando/Accent combinations**: sfz, sf, fz all indicate sudden emphasis. Can be applied within any dynamic context.

## Ornament Terms

Decorative melodic elaborations.

**Trillo** (trill, tr): rapid alternation between written note and note above (usually diatonic step). Duration and speed context-dependent. Baroque: begin on upper note. Classical/Romantic: begin on written note. Always end on written note. Nachschlag: closing turn at end of trill.
**Mordent**: rapid alternation with adjacent note. Upper mordent (inverted mordent): main note - upper note - main note. Lower mordent: main note - lower note - main note. Single alternation (vs trill's sustained alternation).
**Gruppetto / Turn**: four-note figure: upper neighbor - main note - lower neighbor - main note. Notated with S-like symbol. Placed over note: begins with upper neighbor. Placed after note: begins on main note then ornaments.
**Appoggiatura**: "leaning note." Accented non-chord tone on the beat, resolving stepwise. Takes time value from the main note (typically half). Expressive dissonance. Notated as small note before main note (with no slash).
**Acciaccatura**: "crushed note." Very short grace note before the beat (or on the beat, quickly released). Notated as small note with slash through stem. Brief, unaccented. Shorter and less significant than appoggiatura.
**Grace note**: general term for small notes preceding a main note. Encompasses both appoggiatura and acciaccatura.
**Cadenza**: improvised or written-out solo passage, typically near the end of a movement in a concerto. Displays virtuosity. Fermata on dominant chord, soloist plays freely, orchestra re-enters.
**Fioritura**: florid ornamentation. Extended ornamental passage, particularly in vocal music. Displays vocal agility.
**Glissando** (gliss.): slide between two pitches. On piano: finger slides across keys (all notes sounded). On strings: finger slides on string (continuous pitch). On trombone: slide (true continuous glissando). On other instruments: rapid scale-like passage.
**Tremolo**: rapid repetition of a single note (bowed tremolo on strings, repeated notes on keyboard) or rapid alternation between two notes (fingered tremolo). Notated with slashes through stem.
**Vibrato**: regular pitch oscillation above and below the written note. Standard in most post-Baroque performance. Width and speed vary by era, style, and instrument. Not strictly an ornament in modern practice - a fundamental aspect of tone production.
**Nachschlag**: closing ornament at end of trill. Two notes (written note, lower neighbor) played quickly at trill's conclusion.
**Pralltriller**: short trill. Brief upper-note trill, typically 3-4 notes. Begins on written note in modern practice.
**Schleifer**: slide. Ascending grace-note figure leading to main note.

## Structural & Form Terms

### Navigation & Repeat Terms

**Da Capo** (D.C.): from the head. Repeat from the very beginning.
**Da Capo al Fine** (D.C. al Fine): repeat from beginning, play to "Fine" (end mark).
**Da Capo al Coda** (D.C. al Coda): repeat from beginning, at coda sign jump to Coda section.
**Dal Segno** (D.S.): from the sign. Repeat from the segno (S-like symbol).
**Dal Segno al Fine** (D.S. al Fine): repeat from segno, play to Fine.
**Dal Segno al Coda** (D.S. al Coda): repeat from segno, at coda sign jump to Coda.
**Segno**: the sign marking a repeat point.
**Fine**: the end. Marks where piece ends after D.C. or D.S. repeat.
**Coda**: tail. A concluding section, often after the main structural repeat.
**Codetta**: small coda. Brief concluding passage within a larger section.
**Attacca**: attack immediately. Proceed to next movement/section without pause.
**Segue**: follow, continue. Proceed without break (similar to attacca, sometimes implying continuation of style/pattern).
**Prima volta** (1st ending): first-time ending bracket. Play this ending first time through repeat.
**Seconda volta** (2nd ending): second-time ending bracket. Play this ending on the repeat.
**Bis**: twice. Repeat the passage.
**G.P.** (General Pause): all performers silent. Complete silence in all parts simultaneously.
**Lunga** (lunga pausa): long pause. Extended silence or fermata held longer than normal.
**Fermata**: hold. Note or rest sustained beyond its written value. Duration at performer's/conductor's discretion. Corona in some traditions.

### Large Form Designations

**Symphony**: multi-movement orchestral work. Typically 4 movements: fast (sonata) - slow - dance (minuet/scherzo) - fast (rondo/sonata).
**Concerto**: work for soloist(s) with orchestra. Typically 3 movements: fast - slow - fast.
**Sonata**: multi-movement work for solo instrument or small ensemble. Implies sonata-form first movement.
**Suite**: collection of dance movements or character pieces. Baroque: allemande-courante-sarabande-gigue. Modern: any grouped set.
**Overture**: orchestral introduction. Opera overture, concert overture (standalone piece).
**Fantasia**: free-form composition. Not bound by strict formal conventions.
**Rhapsody**: free-form, often dramatic/emotional. Frequently incorporates folk material.
**Nocturne**: night piece. Lyrical, contemplative. Chopin codified the form.
**Etude**: study. Technically demanding, designed to develop specific skill. Chopin/Liszt elevated to concert works.
**Prelude**: introductory piece. Originally preceded fugue (Bach WTC). Later: standalone short character piece (Chopin, Debussy).
**Fugue**: contrapuntal form. Subject stated in each voice successively. Episodes, strettos, modulations.
**Scherzo**: joke. Fast, triple-meter movement replacing minuet (Beethoven). Chopin: independent dramatic works.
**Minuet**: moderate triple-meter dance. Standard third movement in Classical era. Minuet and Trio form (ABA).
**Rondo**: recurring theme alternating with episodes (ABACA or ABACABA).
**March**: processional music in duple meter. Military, funeral, ceremonial.
**Waltz**: triple-meter dance. Emphasis on beat 1.
**Polonaise**: Polish dance in triple meter. Stately, accented.
**Mazurka**: Polish dance in triple meter. Accent on beat 2 or 3 (unusual).
**Ballade**: narrative form. Chopin: large-scale dramatic piano works.
**Impromptu**: quasi-improvised character piece. Schubert, Chopin.
**Intermezzo**: between-piece. Originally: interlude between acts. Brahms: short piano character piece.
**Capriccio**: capricious, free-form. Lively, unpredictable character.
**Toccata**: touch piece. Display of keyboard technique. Rapid, virtuosic.
**Passacaglia**: variations over repeated bass line (ground bass). Baroque origin.
**Chaconne**: variations over repeated chord progression. Related to passacaglia.
**Theme and Variations**: theme stated, then modified through successive variations.
**Mass**: sacred choral work. Ordinary: Kyrie, Gloria, Credo, Sanctus, Agnus Dei.
**Requiem**: mass for the dead. Dies Irae, Lacrimosa, Lux Aeterna.
**Oratorio**: large sacred/dramatic work for chorus, soloists, orchestra. Unstaged (vs opera).
**Cantata**: vocal work with instrumental accompaniment. Sacred (Bach) or secular.
**Opera**: staged dramatic work combining singing, acting, orchestra. Aria, recitative, ensemble, chorus.
**Lied** (pl. Lieder): German art song for voice and piano. Schubert, Schumann, Brahms, Wolf.
**Art song**: concert song for voice and piano (broader term than Lied). Melodie (French), romanza (Italian).
**Chamber music**: small ensemble (2-9 players), one player per part. String quartet = foundational form.
**String quartet**: two violins, viola, cello. The "purest" form of Western chamber music.

### Texture Terms

**Monophonic**: single melodic line, no accompaniment. Gregorian chant. Unison ensemble.
**Homophonic**: melody with chordal accompaniment. Most common texture in Western music.
**Polyphonic**: multiple independent melodic lines simultaneously. Counterpoint. Bach fugues.
**Heterophonic**: same melody performed simultaneously with variation by different performers. Common in non-Western traditions.
**Homorhythmic**: all voices/parts move in the same rhythm. Hymn-like. Chordal texture.
**Contrapuntal**: characterized by counterpoint. Independent melodic lines. Imitative or free.
**Antiphonal**: alternating between groups (choirs, ensembles). Call and response between separated forces.
**Canon**: strict imitation. One voice followed by another at fixed interval/delay. Round = perpetual canon at unison.
**Fugue**: most developed contrapuntal form. Subject, answer, countersubject, episodes, stretto.

## Interval & Chord Terminology

### Intervals

**Unison** (P1): same pitch. 0 semitones.
**Minor second** (m2): 1 semitone. Dissonant, tense. Half step.
**Major second** (M2): 2 semitones. Mildly dissonant. Whole step.
**Minor third** (m3): 3 semitones. Defines minor quality. Sad, dark.
**Major third** (M3): 4 semitones. Defines major quality. Bright, happy.
**Perfect fourth** (P4): 5 semitones. Consonant but contextually dissonant (in traditional counterpoint, treated as dissonance against bass).
**Tritone** (A4/d5): 6 semitones. Augmented fourth / diminished fifth. Maximum instability. "Diabolus in musica." Historically avoided, now essential (dominant 7th chord, diminished chords, blues).
**Perfect fifth** (P5): 7 semitones. Most consonant interval after unison/octave. Foundation of tonal harmony. Power chord = root + fifth.
**Minor sixth** (m6): 8 semitones.
**Major sixth** (M6): 9 semitones.
**Minor seventh** (m7): 10 semitones. Dominant 7th character. Blues, jazz essential.
**Major seventh** (M7): 11 semitones. Lush, modern, jazz harmony.
**Octave** (P8): 12 semitones. Same pitch class, different register. Maximum consonance.
**Compound intervals**: intervals exceeding an octave. 9th = octave + 2nd. 11th = octave + 4th. 13th = octave + 6th. Common in jazz/extended harmony.

### Interval Qualities

**Perfect**: unison, 4th, 5th, octave. Can be augmented or diminished.
**Major/Minor**: 2nd, 3rd, 6th, 7th. Major raised half-step = augmented. Minor lowered half-step = diminished.
**Augmented**: half-step larger than major or perfect.
**Diminished**: half-step smaller than minor or perfect.

### Chord Types

**Major triad**: root, M3, P5. Bright, stable.
**Minor triad**: root, m3, P5. Dark, melancholic.
**Diminished triad**: root, m3, d5 (tritone). Unstable, tense.
**Augmented triad**: root, M3, A5. Unsettled, symmetrical (divides octave in 3 equal M3s).
**Suspended 2nd** (sus2): root, M2, P5. Open, ambiguous. Neither major nor minor.
**Suspended 4th** (sus4): root, P4, P5. Tense, wants to resolve to major or minor.
**Power chord** (5): root, P5 only. No third = ambiguous major/minor. Rock/metal fundamental.
**Dominant 7th**: major triad + m7. Tense, demands resolution to tonic. Foundation of functional harmony.
**Major 7th**: major triad + M7. Lush, jazzy, modern.
**Minor 7th**: minor triad + m7. Warm, common in jazz/pop.
**Half-diminished 7th** (m7b5): diminished triad + m7. Jazz ii chord in minor. Ambiguous, tense.
**Diminished 7th**: diminished triad + d7 (enharmonic M6). Symmetrical (divides octave in 4 equal m3s). Can resolve to 4 different keys. Maximum tension.
**Augmented 7th**: augmented triad + m7. Altered dominant sound.
**Add chords**: triad + added note without 7th. Add9 = triad + 9th (no 7th).
**Extended chords**: 9th (7+9), 11th (7+9+11), 13th (7+9+11+13). Jazz standard voicings often omit 5th, sometimes 9th or 11th.
**Altered chords**: dominant 7th with altered tensions (b9, #9, b5/#11, #5/b13). Maximum tension before resolution.
**Slash chords**: chord over non-root bass note. C/E = C major with E in bass (first inversion).
**Polychords**: two triads stacked. C major over F# major = Petrushka chord (Stravinsky).

## Performance Direction Terms

### String-Specific
**Arco**: with the bow. Return to bowing after pizzicato.
**Pizzicato** (pizz.): plucked with finger.
**Col legno**: with the wood (of the bow). Battuto (struck) or tratto (drawn).
**Sul tasto**: over the fingerboard. Soft, muted tone.
**Sul ponticello** (sul pont.): near the bridge. Ethereal, overtone-rich.
**Con sordino** (con sord.): with mute.
**Senza sordino** (senza sord.): without mute.
**Divisi** (div.): divided. Section splits into multiple parts.
**Unisono** (unis.): in unison. Section plays same notes together.
**A due** (a 2): two players on same part. Opposite of divisi.
**Tremolo**: rapid bow repetition (bowed tremolo) or rapid alternation between notes (fingered tremolo).

### Keyboard-Specific
**Una corda** (U.C.): one string. Left (soft) pedal on piano. Shifts hammers to strike fewer strings.
**Tre corde** (T.C.): three strings. Release soft pedal.
**Con pedale** (con ped.): with sustain pedal.
**Senza pedale**: without pedal.
**Sostenuto**: sustain selected notes (middle pedal on grand piano).

### Vocal-Specific
**A cappella**: without instrumental accompaniment. Voices only.
**Recitativo**: speech-like singing following natural text rhythm. Secco (with minimal accompaniment) or accompagnato (with orchestra).
**Aria**: extended solo vocal piece within opera/oratorio. Showcases voice.
**Parlando**: speaking manner. Sung with speech-like delivery.
**Sprechgesang / Sprechstimme**: speech-song. Half-spoken, half-sung. Schoenberg.
**Melisma**: multiple notes sung on single syllable.
**Syllabic**: one note per syllable.
**Coloratura**: elaborate vocal ornamentation. Rapid runs, trills, leaps.

### General Performance
**Ad libitum** (ad lib.): at liberty. Free tempo, optional inclusion, or improvisation.
**A piacere**: at pleasure. Performer's discretion regarding tempo/interpretation.
**Obbligato**: obligatory. An essential accompanying part that cannot be omitted (historically, opposite of ad libitum).
**Ripieno**: filling. The full ensemble (vs. concertino/solo group) in Baroque concerto grosso.
**Soli**: plural of solo. Small group plays (not full section).
**Tutti**: everyone. Full ensemble plays.
**Tacet**: be silent. Performer does not play during this section/movement.
**Volta**: time. Prima volta = first time. Seconda volta = second time.
**Opus** (Op.): work. Catalog number for a composer's published works. Op. 1 = first published work.
**Ossia**: or, alternatively. An alternate (usually easier) version of a passage.

## French Musical Terms

Common French terms, particularly in Impressionist and 20th-century French music.

**Animez**: enliven, become animated. Speed up with energy.
**Assez**: enough, fairly. Assez vif = fairly lively.
**Au mouvement**: return to tempo (equivalent of a tempo).
**Avec**: with. Avec expression = with expression.
**Beaucoup**: much. Sans beaucoup de nuances = without much nuance.
**Cédez**: yield, slow down slightly.
**Doux**: sweet, gentle, soft.
**En**: in. En dehors = standing out, brought out (important voice).
**En dehors**: brought out, prominent. Mark the melodic line.
**Et**: and.
**Fort**: strong, loud.
**Lent**: slow.
**Léger**: light.
**Modéré**: moderate.
**Mouvement** (mouvt.): tempo. Au mouvement = a tempo.
**Peu**: little. Un peu = a little.
**Plus**: more. Plus vite = faster.
**Presser**: press forward, accelerate.
**Ralentir**: slow down.
**Retenir**: hold back.
**Retenu**: held back (slower tempo achieved).
**Sans**: without. Sans sourdine = without mute.
**Très**: very. Très lent = very slow.
**Un peu**: a little.
**Vif**: lively, quick.
**Vite**: fast.
