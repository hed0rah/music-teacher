# Modes, Scales & Progressions: Comprehensive Reference

## The Seven Diatonic Modes (Church Modes)

All seven modes derived from the same pitch set (white keys on piano starting from different notes). Each mode has a unique interval pattern creating distinct mood and harmonic possibilities. Characteristic tone(s) = the note(s) that distinguish it from parallel major or minor.

---

### Ionian (I) - The Major Scale

**Formula**: 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 (W-W-H-W-W-W-H)
**Characteristic**: no altered tones vs major. This IS the major scale.
**Mood**: bright, resolved, stable, happy, "default" Western tonality.
**Avoid note**: 4th degree (when sustained over tonic chord, creates dissonance against 3rd).
**Diatonic chords**: I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, vii-dim
**Usage**: default major key context. Most Western tonal music. Pop, classical, folk.
**Modal vamp**: I - IV or I - V (standard tonal progressions reinforce Ionian naturally).

---

### Dorian (ii) - Minor with Raised 6th

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 5 6 b7 (W-H-W-W-W-H-W)
**Characteristic tone**: natural 6th (major 6th over a minor chord). This is what distinguishes Dorian from Aeolian (natural minor). The raised 6th creates a warmer, less dark minor quality.
**Mood**: warm minor, jazzy, sophisticated melancholy, hopeful darkness. Not as bleak as Aeolian.
**Avoid note**: 6th degree can sound odd if overemphasized in certain melodic contexts, but it's also the characteristic tone. Context-dependent.
**Diatonic chords**: i, ii, bIII, IV, v, vi-dim, bVII
**Key chord**: IV major (in a minor context, having a major IV is uniquely Dorian).
**Usage**: modal jazz (Miles Davis "So What"), Santana, Daft Punk "Get Lucky", folk, medieval. Extremely common in film and game scoring for "neutral minor" quality.
**Modal vamp**: i - IV (the IV major chord is the Dorian identifier. Play Dm - G and you're in D Dorian).
**Compared to Aeolian**: only difference is 6th degree (natural vs flat). Dorian = brighter, warmer minor.

---

### Phrygian (iii) - Minor with Flattened 2nd

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 (H-W-W-W-H-W-W)
**Characteristic tone**: b2 (half-step above root). This creates the exotic, dark, "Spanish" quality. The b2 pulling down to the root creates strong gravitational resolution.
**Mood**: dark, exotic, Spanish/flamenco, Middle Eastern, aggressive. The darkest commonly-used diatonic mode.
**Avoid note**: b2 is characteristic but can destabilize if used carelessly in melodic lines. Use with intent.
**Diatonic chords**: i, bII, bIII, iv, v-dim, bVI, bvii
**Key chord**: bII major (the Phrygian cadence: bII - i is the defining sound).
**Usage**: flamenco, metal (especially death/black metal), Middle Eastern influenced music, film scoring for threat/exoticism. Metallica, Iron Maiden. Classical: Phrygian cadence (iv6 - V in minor key context).
**Modal vamp**: i - bII (Em - F in E Phrygian. The half-step bass motion IS the Phrygian sound).
**The Andalusian cadence**: i - bVII - bVI - V (Am - G - F - E in A Phrygian context). One of the most recognizable progressions in Western music.

---

### Lydian (IV) - Major with Raised 4th

**Formula**: 1 2 3 #4 5 6 7 (W-W-W-H-W-W-H)
**Characteristic tone**: #4 (augmented 4th / tritone above root). Creates floating, ethereal, "brighter than bright" quality. The #4 eliminates the only "avoid note" in Ionian.
**Mood**: bright, ethereal, dreamy, floating, wondrous, magical. Brighter and more open than Ionian.
**Avoid note**: none. Lydian is the only diatonic mode with no traditional avoid notes. Every note is consonant against the tonic chord. This is why film composers love it.
**Diatonic chords**: I, II, iii, #iv-dim, V, vi, vii
**Key chord**: II major (major chord on 2nd degree is uniquely Lydian).
**Usage**: film scoring extensively (John Williams - E.T., Star Wars "Yoda's Theme", Flying themes). Simpsons theme. Joe Satriani. Creates sense of wonder, flight, magic. Bartok used Lydian extensively (Hungarian folk music connection via Lydian-dominant).
**Modal vamp**: I - II (C - D in C Lydian. The whole-step bass motion with both chords major = instant Lydian).
**George Russell's Lydian Chromatic Concept**: argued Lydian (not Ionian) is the "true" tonal center because it's the mode generated by stacking perfect fifths from the root.

---

### Mixolydian (V) - Major with Flattened 7th

**Formula**: 1 2 3 4 5 6 b7 (W-W-H-W-W-H-W)
**Characteristic tone**: b7 (minor 7th in a major context). Creates bluesy, dominant quality. The b7 removes the leading tone, eliminating the strong pull to resolve.
**Mood**: bluesy major, rock, folk, open, earthy. Major quality without the strong resolution tendency of Ionian.
**Avoid note**: 4th degree (same as Ionian - creates tension against 3rd over tonic).
**Diatonic chords**: I, ii, bIII-dim (rare), IV, v, vi, bVII
**Key chord**: bVII major (major chord on flatted 7th is uniquely Mixolydian).
**Usage**: rock (AC/DC, Rolling Stones, Beatles "Norwegian Wood"), blues, folk, Celtic music, jam band. The dominant 7th chord IS Mixolydian. Any blues-rock riff sitting on a dominant 7th chord is Mixolydian.
**Modal vamp**: I - bVII (G - F in G Mixolydian. "Sweet Home Alabama" territory).
**Relationship to blues**: blues = Mixolydian + blue notes (b3, b5). The b7 in a major context = the foundation of blues harmony.

---

### Aeolian (vi) - The Natural Minor Scale

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 (W-H-W-W-H-W-W)
**Characteristic**: this IS the natural minor scale. b3, b6, b7.
**Mood**: melancholic, dark, reflective, sad. The standard "minor" sound.
**Avoid note**: b6 can sound heavy against the 5th (creates minor 2nd in upper register). Not strictly avoided but requires care.
**Diatonic chords**: i, ii-dim, bIII, iv, v, bVI, bVII
**Key chord**: bVI major (distinguishes from Dorian which has vi-dim instead).
**Usage**: default minor key in pop, rock, classical. Vast majority of "sad" or "dark" music. Often mixed with harmonic minor (raised 7th for dominant V chord) in practice.
**Modal vamp**: i - bVI or i - bVII (Am - F or Am - G in A Aeolian).
**In practice**: pure Aeolian is relatively rare in classical music (harmonic and melodic minor are more common for their leading tone). Very common in pop/rock/metal where the v minor chord (instead of V major) is used.

---

### Locrian (vii) - Diminished Quality

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 b5 b6 b7 (H-W-W-H-W-W-W)
**Characteristic tone**: b5 (diminished 5th / tritone above root). The tonic triad itself is diminished, making Locrian inherently unstable.
**Mood**: extremely unstable, dissonant, unresolved. Cannot establish a convincing tonal center because the tonic chord is diminished.
**Avoid note**: the b5 is both characteristic and destabilizing. The entire mode resists being "home."
**Diatonic chords**: i-dim, bII, biii, iv, bV, bVI, bvii
**Usage**: rarely used as a key center. Theoretical importance. Some metal (Bjork "Army of Me" intro is Locrian-adjacent). Used over half-diminished chords in jazz (Locrian #2 from melodic minor preferred). Black Sabbath riffs occasionally Locrian-flavored.
**Modal vamp**: difficult to establish. i-dim - bII is the closest to a functional Locrian cadence but the bII constantly pulls attention away from the diminished tonic.

## Modes of Melodic Minor (Jazz Minor)

Seven modes derived from the ascending melodic minor scale (1 2 b3 4 5 6 7). Each mode has unique applications, particularly in jazz harmony.

### 1. Melodic Minor (Jazz Minor)

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 5 6 7
**Character**: minor triad with major 7th. The minor scale with a leading tone AND major 6th. Darker than major, brighter than natural minor.
**Usage**: over min(maj7) chords. Jazz minor tonality. Film noir quality.

### 2. Dorian b2 (Phrygian #6)

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 5 6 b7
**Character**: Dorian with a Phrygian flavor (b2). Exotic.
**Usage**: over sus4(b9) chords. Uncommon but distinctive.

### 3. Lydian Augmented

**Formula**: 1 2 3 #4 #5 6 7
**Character**: Lydian with raised 5th. Even more "floating" than standard Lydian. Otherworldly.
**Usage**: over maj7#5 chords. Dreamy, surreal quality.

### 4. Lydian Dominant (Lydian b7 / Overtone Scale)

**Formula**: 1 2 3 #4 5 6 b7
**Character**: Lydian brightness + dominant 7th tension. The acoustic/overtone scale (approximates natural harmonic series). Bartok scale.
**Usage**: over dominant 7th chords (especially #11 voicings). Extremely useful in jazz. The "Simpsons scale." Bartok used extensively.
**Important**: this is the 4th mode of melodic minor but functions as a dominant chord scale with Lydian color.

### 5. Mixolydian b6 (Aeolian Dominant / Hindu Scale)

**Formula**: 1 2 3 4 5 b6 b7
**Character**: major triad with b6 and b7. Creates tension between major 3rd and dark upper structure.
**Usage**: over dominant chords resolving to minor. Melodic minor harmony.

### 6. Locrian #2 (Half-Diminished Scale)

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 b5 b6 b7
**Character**: Locrian but with natural 2nd (less harsh than standard Locrian).
**Usage**: the preferred scale over half-diminished (m7b5) chords in jazz. More usable than standard Locrian.

### 7. Super Locrian (Altered Scale / Altered Dominant)

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 b4(3) b5 b6 b7
**Character**: every possible tension is altered. Maximum dissonance over a dominant chord.
**Usage**: over altered dominant chords (7b9#9b5#5). THE jazz altered dominant sound. Resolves to I or i. Contains b9, #9, b5 (#11), and #5 (b13) - all four altered tensions.
**Critical jazz application**: V7alt - Imaj7 or V7alt - i. The most tension-filled dominant resolution possible.

## Modes of Harmonic Minor

Seven modes derived from the harmonic minor scale (1 2 b3 4 5 b6 7). The augmented 2nd interval (b6 to 7) gives these modes their distinctive exotic quality.

### 1. Harmonic Minor

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 7
**Character**: the "classical" minor sound. Augmented 2nd between b6 and 7 creates exotic, Middle Eastern flavor. The raised 7th provides a leading tone for strong V-i cadence.
**Usage**: classical minor key harmony (V7 - i requires the raised 7th). Film scoring for exotic or dramatic minor.

### 2. Locrian #6

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 b5 6 b7
**Usage**: rare. Over diminished-type chords.

### 3. Ionian #5 (Augmented Major)

**Formula**: 1 2 3 4 #5 6 7
**Usage**: over augmented major 7th chords. Dreamy, unstable.

### 4. Dorian #4 (Ukrainian Dorian / Romanian Scale)

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 #4 5 6 b7
**Character**: Dorian with raised 4th creates distinctive Eastern European flavor. The #4 + b3 combination = exotic.
**Usage**: klezmer, Romanian/Ukrainian folk, some metal. Distinctive "gypsy" quality (acknowledging problematic term - Romani music).

### 5. Phrygian Dominant (Spanish Phrygian / Freygish / Hijaz)

**Formula**: 1 b2 3 4 5 b6 b7
**Character**: major 3rd with Phrygian b2. Creates powerful exotic dominant sound. The augmented 2nd between b2 and 3 is the defining interval.
**Mood**: Spanish, Middle Eastern, Jewish liturgical, intensely dramatic.
**Usage**: flamenco (THE flamenco scale), Jewish music (Hava Nagila, Freygish mode), Arabic music (Maqam Hijaz), metal, film scoring for exotic/dramatic. Extremely versatile and recognizable.
**One of the most important modes outside the diatonic seven**. Used over dominant chords resolving to minor (V7b9 - i).

### 6. Lydian #2

**Formula**: 1 #2 3 #4 5 6 7
**Usage**: rare. Exotic major quality.

### 7. Ultra Locrian (Altered Diminished)

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 b4 b5 b6 bb7
**Usage**: theoretical. Over diminished 7th chords.

## Pentatonic & Blues Scales

### Major Pentatonic

**Formula**: 1 2 3 5 6 (5 notes, no 4th or 7th)
**Character**: universally bright, open, consonant. Found independently in virtually every world music tradition. Removing the 4th and 7th eliminates all semitone tensions.
**Usage**: folk music worldwide, rock solos, pop melodies, country, Celtic. "Amazing Grace." Always sounds "right" over its parent key.

### Minor Pentatonic

**Formula**: 1 b3 4 5 b7 (5 notes)
**Character**: the foundation of rock and blues soloing. Dark, powerful, universally applicable over minor contexts.
**Usage**: rock, blues, metal, funk. The first scale most guitarists learn. Works over minor chords, dominant 7th chords, and even some major contexts (creates tension).

### Blues Scale

**Formula**: 1 b3 4 b5 5 b7 (minor pentatonic + b5 "blue note")
**Character**: the b5 (blue note) adds chromatic tension. Bent between b3/3 and 4/b5 creates the essential blues inflection.
**Usage**: blues, rock, jazz-blues, funk. The blue note is often bent/slid through rather than landed on. The soul of blues expression is in the microtonal space between b3-3 and b5-5.

### Major Blues Scale

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 3 5 6 (major pentatonic + b3)
**Character**: major pentatonic with blue note (b3). Bright but with blues inflection.
**Usage**: country-blues, jazz, combining major brightness with blues feel.

### Japanese Pentatonic Scales

**Hirajoshi**: 1 2 b3 5 b6. Minor pentatonic variant with b6 instead of b7. Dark, Japanese koto music.
**In**: 1 b2 4 5 b6. Very dark, atmospheric. Phrygian-derived pentatonic.
**Yo**: 1 2 4 5 6. Bright Japanese folk scale. No semitones.
**Iwato**: 1 b2 4 b5 b7. Extremely dark, dissonant. Contains tritone.
**Kumoi**: 1 2 b3 5 6. Similar to Hirajoshi but with natural 6th. Gentler.

## Symmetric Scales

Scales with repeating interval patterns. Limited transpositions due to symmetry.

### Whole Tone Scale

**Formula**: 1 2 3 #4 #5 b7 (all whole steps)
**Character**: dreamy, floating, ambiguous. No semitones = no tension/resolution. No perfect 5th = no tonal anchor. Every note equidistant.
**Only 2 transpositions exist** (C whole tone and Db whole tone cover all 12 pitches).
**Usage**: Debussy extensively. Impressionism. Transition passages. Dream sequences in film. Over augmented chords or dominant 7#5.

### Diminished Scale (Whole-Half)

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 4 b5 b6 6 7 (alternating whole and half steps)
**Character**: 8-note scale (octatonic). Symmetrical - repeats every minor 3rd.
**Only 3 transpositions exist**.
**Usage**: over diminished 7th chords. Messiaen (Mode 2). Stravinsky, Bartok, Ravel. Creates kaleidoscopic harmonic ambiguity.

### Dominant Diminished (Half-Whole)

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 3 #4 5 6 b7 (alternating half and whole steps)
**Character**: dominant 7th chord with all available tensions (b9, #9, #11, 13). Rich, tense, colorful.
**Only 3 transpositions exist**.
**Usage**: over dominant 7th chords with altered tensions. Jazz standard. Creates the "outside" dominant sound.

### Augmented Scale

**Formula**: 1 b3 3 5 #5 7 (alternating m3 and half steps)
**Character**: symmetrical, 6 notes. Combines two augmented triads a half-step apart.
**Only 4 transpositions exist**.
**Usage**: over augmented chords. Creates shimmering, ambiguous quality. Coltrane, John Patitucci.

### Chromatic Scale

**Formula**: all 12 semitones
**Character**: maximum pitch density. No tonal center by default. Contains all possible intervals.
**Usage**: chromatic passages, chromatic voice leading, approach notes, total chromaticism in atonal music.

## Exotic & World Scales

### Hungarian Minor

**Formula**: 1 2 b3 #4 5 b6 7
**Character**: two augmented 2nds (#4-5 gap is whole step, but b3-#4 and b6-7 are augmented 2nds). Extremely exotic, Eastern European.
**Usage**: Hungarian/Romani music, film scoring for Eastern European settings, metal.

### Hungarian Major

**Formula**: 1 #2 3 #4 5 6 b7
**Character**: major with raised 2nd and 4th, lowered 7th. Bartok scale.
**Usage**: Bartok, Eastern European folk-influenced composition.

### Double Harmonic Major (Byzantine / Arabic)

**Formula**: 1 b2 3 4 5 b6 7
**Character**: two augmented 2nds (b2-3 and b6-7). Intensely exotic. No "Western" equivalent.
**Usage**: Middle Eastern music, Byzantine chant, Dick Dale "Misirlou." Metal (Marty Friedman).

### Neapolitan Major

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 5 6 7
**Character**: minor scale with b2 and major 7th. The Neapolitan chord (bII) derives from this scale.
**Usage**: classical (Neapolitan 6th chord usage), film scoring for gravity/drama.

### Neapolitan Minor

**Formula**: 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 7
**Character**: similar to harmonic minor but with b2. Very dark.
**Usage**: classical Neapolitan school, dramatic film scoring.

### Enigmatic Scale

**Formula**: 1 b2 3 #4 #5 #6 7
**Character**: Verdi invented this. Unusual, difficult to categorize. Tonal ambiguity.
**Usage**: rare. Compositional curiosity. Verdi "Ave Maria."

### Persian Scale

**Formula**: 1 b2 3 4 b5 b6 7
**Character**: dark, exotic, contains tritone. Middle Eastern.
**Usage**: Persian classical music influences, film scoring.

### Bebop Scales

**Bebop dominant**: Mixolydian + natural 7th (8 notes: 1 2 3 4 5 6 b7 7). The added passing tone allows chord tones to fall on downbeats during eighth-note lines.
**Bebop major**: Ionian + #5 (8 notes: 1 2 3 4 5 #5 6 7).
**Bebop Dorian**: Dorian + natural 3rd (8 notes: 1 2 b3 3 4 5 6 b7).
**Usage**: jazz improvisation. The 8th note ensures chord tones align with strong beats. Charlie Parker, Dizzy Gillespie.

### Prometheus Scale (Scriabin's Mystic Chord)

**Formula**: 1 2 3 #4 6 b7
**Character**: derived from Scriabin's "mystic chord." Whole-tone adjacent but with unique character.
**Usage**: Scriabin late works, mystical/transcendent quality.

## Chord Progressions: Functional Harmony

### Foundational Progressions

**I - IV - V - I**: the most basic tonal progression. Tonic establishes home, subdominant creates departure, dominant creates tension, tonic resolves. Foundation of Western harmony. Blues, rock, folk, hymns.

**I - V - vi - IV**: the "Axis of Awesome" progression. The most common pop progression of the last 40 years. Let It Be, No Woman No Cry, With or Without You, Don't Stop Believin', countless others. Can start on any chord in the cycle.

**vi - IV - I - V**: same four chords rotated. "Sensitive" rotation. Despacito, someone Like You.

**I - vi - IV - V**: 50s/doo-wop progression. Stand By Me, Every Breath You Take, Earth Angel.

**I - IV - vi - V**: common pop variant.

**I - V - IV**: rock/blues standard. Born in the USA, La Bamba, Twist and Shout.

**ii - V - I**: jazz foundational cadence. The most important progression in jazz harmony. Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7 in C major. ii provides subdominant function, V provides dominant tension, I resolves. Endless variations and substitutions built on this framework.

**I - bVII - IV**: Mixolydian rock progression. Sweet Home Alabama, Hey Jude (partial). Lydian-Mixolydian ambiguity.

### Jazz Progressions

**ii - V - I** (major): Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7. The foundational jazz cadence.
**ii - V - i** (minor): Dm7b5 - G7b9 - Cm(maj7). Minor key ii-V-i. Half-diminished ii, altered dominant V.

**iii - vi - ii - V - I**: extended circle of fifths descent. Full turnaround. "Autumn Leaves" harmonic foundation.

**I - vi - ii - V** (Rhythm Changes A section): Bbmaj7 - Gm7 - Cm7 - F7. From Gershwin "I Got Rhythm." Standard jam session form. Bebop test piece.

**Coltrane Changes (Giant Steps changes)**: movement by major thirds (dividing the octave into 3 equal parts). Bmaj7 - D7 - Gmaj7 - Bb7 - Ebmaj7 - F#7 - Bmaj7. Three tonal centers a major 3rd apart, each approached by its V7. Maximum harmonic motion. Revolutionary in 1959.

**Bird Blues (jazz blues)**: standard 12-bar blues with ii-V substitutions.
| Fmaj7 | Em7b5 A7b9 | Dm7 G7 | Cm7 F7 |
| Bbmaj7 | Bbm7 Eb7 | Fmaj7 D7 | Gm7 C7 |
| Am7 D7 | Gm7 C7 | Fmaj7 D7 | Gm7 C7 |

**Modal jazz vamps**: static harmony, single chord for extended periods. Dm7 for 16 bars (So What A section). Reduces harmonic rhythm to zero, focuses on melodic invention within the mode.

**Jazz turnarounds**: I - vi - ii - V (standard), I - VI7 - ii - V (with secondary dominant), I - #Idim7 - ii - V (chromatic passing diminished), iii - VI7 - ii - V (with iii substitution for I).

### Blues Progressions

**Standard 12-bar blues**:
| I7 | I7 | I7 | I7 |
| IV7 | IV7 | I7 | I7 |
| V7 | IV7 | I7 | V7 |

**Quick change**: IV7 in bar 2 (instead of staying on I7).
**Minor blues**: same structure with minor chords. i7 - iv7 - v7 (or V7 for stronger cadence).
**8-bar blues**: compressed form. Common in early blues and folk blues.

### Classical Progressions

**Circle of fifths**: I - IV - vii - iii - vi - ii - V - I. Each chord's root is a fifth below the next. The gravitational pull of Western harmony. Bach used this obsessively.

**Romanesca**: I - V - vi - III - IV - I - IV - V. Renaissance/early Baroque standard bass pattern. Pachelbel Canon uses a variant.

**Lament bass (descending tetrachord)**: chromatic descent from tonic to dominant (1 - 7 - b7 - 6 - b6 - 5). Over this bass: i - V6/4 - iv6 - V. Dido's Lament (Purcell), Crucifixus (Bach B minor Mass). Universal grief signifier.

**Pachelbel progression**: I - V - vi - iii - IV - I - IV - V. One of the most recycled progressions in music history. Canon in D.

**Andalusian cadence**: i - bVII - bVI - V (Am - G - F - E in A minor/Phrygian). Flamenco foundation. "Hit the Road Jack." Descending bass, major V chord creates Phrygian dominant flavor at cadence.

## Modal Harmony & Progressions

### Staying in a Mode

The challenge with modal harmony: how to establish a modal center without the music gravitating toward the relative major or minor. The key is emphasizing the characteristic chord(s) and avoiding progressions that imply functional tonality.

**Dorian**: emphasize i - IV. The IV major chord distinguishes Dorian from Aeolian. Avoid V - i (implies harmonic minor, not Dorian). Vamp: Dm7 - G/D or Dm - G.

**Phrygian**: emphasize i - bII. The bII major chord is uniquely Phrygian. The half-step bass motion is the mode's signature. Vamp: Em - F/E or Em - Fmaj7.

**Lydian**: emphasize I - II. The II major chord is uniquely Lydian. Both chords major with whole-step bass = instant Lydian. Vamp: Cmaj7 - D/C or C - D.

**Mixolydian**: emphasize I - bVII. The bVII major chord distinguishes Mixolydian from Ionian. Avoid leading tone resolution (there is none). Vamp: G - F or G7 - F/G.

### Avoid Notes & Characteristic Tones

**Avoid notes** are scale degrees that, when sustained over the tonic chord, create harsh dissonances (typically a minor 9th against a chord tone). They're not "wrong" - they require careful handling.

- Ionian: 4th (minor 9th above 3rd)
- Dorian: b3 can create minor 9th against 9th if both present. Generally avoid-note-free
- Phrygian: b2 (minor 9th above root when sustained, but it's also the characteristic tone)
- Lydian: none (the only diatonic mode with no avoid notes)
- Mixolydian: 4th (same as Ionian)
- Aeolian: b6 (minor 9th above 5th)
- Locrian: b2 and b5 both problematic (root triad is diminished)

## Chord Substitution Techniques

### Tritone Substitution

Replace any dominant 7th chord with the dominant 7th a tritone away. G7 -> Db7. Works because both chords share the same tritone interval (the 3rd and 7th swap roles). Creates chromatic bass motion: Dm7 - Db7 - Cmaj7 instead of Dm7 - G7 - Cmaj7. The most important substitution in jazz.

### Relative Major/Minor Substitution

Replace a chord with its relative: I can be replaced by iii (share 2 of 3 notes). IV can be replaced by vi. V can be replaced by vii (share 2 of 3 notes).

### Diminished Substitution

A diminished 7th chord = 4 potential dominant 7th chords (each note could be the 3rd of a dominant chord a half-step below). Cdim7 functions as C#7, E7, G7, or Bb7 depending on context.

### Upper Structure Triads

Play a triad over a different root/7th chord. E major triad over C7 = C7#9b13. Creates complex extended voicings with simple shapes. Jazz voicing technique.

### Chromatic Approach Chords

Approach target chord from a half-step above or below. Creates chromatic voice leading. Common in jazz and film scoring.

## Modal Interchange (Borrowed Chords)

Borrowing chords from the parallel mode. In C major, borrow chords from C minor (or C Dorian, C Phrygian, etc.).

### Common Borrowed Chords (in major key context)

**bVI** (Ab in C major): borrowed from Aeolian. Dark, dramatic. "Creep" by Radiohead (III - IV - I - I uses bIII and IV). Very common in pop and film.
**bVII** (Bb in C major): borrowed from Mixolydian. Rock staple. Power, darkness without full minor commitment.
**bIII** (Eb in C major): borrowed from Aeolian. Creates parallel minor color in major context.
**iv** (Fm in C major): borrowed from Aeolian. The minor iv in a major key = one of the most emotionally powerful borrowed chords. "My Funny Valentine" cadence. Bittersweet quality.
**i** (Cm in C major): using minor tonic in a major context. Startling, dark.

### Picardy Third

Major I chord at the end of a piece in minor key. Surprise brightness. Baroque convention. Bach frequently ends minor-key works with major tonic.

### "Mario Cadence"

**bVI - bVII - I**: very common in film, games, pop. The bass ascends by whole step to the tonic. Heroic, triumphant quality. Does not use traditional dominant function.

### Backdoor Cadence

**bVII - I** (or bVIImaj7 - Imaj7): approach tonic from a whole step below instead of a fifth above. Common in jazz endings. Creates softer resolution than V-I.

## Extended & Altered Harmony

### Extended Chords

**9th chords**: add 9th (2nd compound) to 7th chord. Major 9 = warm, lush. Minor 9 = cool, jazzy. Dominant 9 = bluesy, funky. Add9 = triad + 9th without 7th (brighter, pop-rock).

**11th chords**: add 11th (4th compound). #11 especially important (Lydian sound over dominant). m11 = dark, modal. Dom 11 = sus-like quality.

**13th chords**: add 13th (6th compound). Dominant 13 = full jazz voicing. Contains all 7 scale tones theoretically. In practice, omit 5th and often 11th.

### Altered Dominants

**b9**: dark, tense. Minor 9th against root. Phrygian/diminished flavor.
**#9**: the "Hendrix chord" (E7#9). Major and minor 3rd simultaneously (the #9 = enharmonic b3). Bluesy, aggressive.
**b5 / #11**: Lydian dominant color. Tritone substitution implied.
**#5 / b13**: augmented dominant. Tense, unresolved.
**Fully altered (7alt)**: all four tensions altered (b9, #9, #11, b13). Maximum pre-resolution tension. Use altered scale (7th mode of melodic minor).

### Quartal Harmony

Chords built in 4ths instead of 3rds. C-F-Bb or D-G-C. Open, modern, ambiguous. No clear major/minor quality. McCoy Tyner signature. Herbie Hancock "Maiden Voyage." Common in modal jazz and contemporary film scoring. Hindemith theorized quartal harmony.

### Quintal Harmony

Chords built in 5ths. C-G-D. Very open, spacious. Power chords are a simple form. Copland "Appalachian Spring" uses quintal voicings for Americana quality.

### Secundal Harmony (Clusters)

Chords built in 2nds. Penderecki, Ligeti, Cowell. Dense, dissonant, textural. At extreme density = cluster chord (covered in REFERENCE_HARMONY_VOICE_LEADING.md).

## Cadences

### Functional Cadences

**Perfect Authentic Cadence** (PAC): V - I with soprano on tonic, both chords in root position. Strongest possible closure. The "full stop" of music.

**Imperfect Authentic Cadence** (IAC): V - I but soprano not on tonic, or inverted chords. Closure but less final. The "comma."

**Half Cadence** (HC): phrase ending on V. Feels incomplete, demands continuation. The "question mark."

**Plagal Cadence** (IV - I): "Amen cadence." Subdominant to tonic. Weaker than authentic but still conclusive. Often used as final gesture after an authentic cadence.

**Deceptive Cadence** (V - vi): expectation for I is subverted. Surprise, extension, emotional redirection. One of the most powerful harmonic devices. Listener expects resolution; gets something else.

**Phrygian Half Cadence** (iv6 - V in minor): half cadence approached by half-step in bass (b6 descending to 5). Dark, final-sounding despite being a half cadence. Common in Baroque.

### Non-Traditional Cadences

**Backdoor Cadence** (bVII - I): approach tonic from whole step below. Jazz endings. Softer than V-I.

**Mario Cadence** (bVI - bVII - I): ascending whole-step bass to tonic via borrowed chords. Triumphant, modal.

**Picardy Third**: minor key piece ending on major I chord. Surprise brightness. Tierce de Picardie.

**Evaded Cadence**: V chord reached but resolution to I is delayed, avoided, or redirected. Creates continuous harmonic motion. Wagner specialty.

**Plagal with minor iv** (iv - I in major): the minor iv chord in major context creates bittersweet closure. Extremely expressive. Common in gospel, soul, Radiohead.

## Voice Leading in Progressions

### Smooth Voice Leading Principles

- **Common tones**: hold shared notes between consecutive chords in the same voice.
- **Stepwise motion**: move remaining voices by the smallest interval possible (half or whole step).
- **Contrary motion in outer voices**: bass and soprano moving in opposite directions creates balance and independence.
- **Avoid parallel 5ths and octaves** between outer voices (traditional rule, relaxed in pop/rock/jazz).
- **Leading tone resolves up** to tonic (in traditional tonal harmony).
- **7th of chord resolves down** by step.

### Neo-Riemannian Transformations

Modern theory describing parsimonious (minimal movement) voice leading between triads:

**L (Leading-tone exchange)**: C major -> E minor. One note moves by half-step (B->C or C->B).
**P (Parallel)**: C major -> C minor. One note moves by half-step (E->Eb or Eb->E).
**R (Relative)**: C major -> A minor. One note moves by whole step (G->A or A->G).

These three operations, combined, describe all possible single-voice-leading-step transformations between major and minor triads. Used in film scoring analysis (Howard Shore, John Williams frequently use L-P-R chains for chromatic but smooth harmonic motion).

### Chromatic Voice Leading

Moving all voices by half-step or holding common tones while one voice moves chromatically. Creates smooth connections between seemingly unrelated chords. Wagner's Tristan chord resolves through chromatic voice leading. Film scoring relies heavily on this for emotional harmonic motion without strict functional logic.
